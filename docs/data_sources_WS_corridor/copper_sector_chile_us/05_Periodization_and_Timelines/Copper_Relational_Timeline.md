# Copper Relational Timeline

## Late colonial / early republic prehistory
- Early US copper formation remains technologically dependent and regionally uneven.
- British/Swansea smelting dominance sets an external benchmark.
- Chilean ores already matter as inputs for emerging US smelting/refining efforts.

## Nineteenth century
- US copper shifts from importer toward major producer and exporter.
- Refining and smelting capacities become increasingly central.
- Chile enters world copper circuits differently across cycles.

## Early twentieth century
- American-controlled mines in South America rise in importance.
- The US becomes more than a producer: it becomes a coordinating and processing hub.

## 1940s–1960s
- US stockpile policy becomes a strategic-state intervention in copper.
- Chilean conflict with foreign firms intensifies over price, taxation, investment, and control.

## 1947–1971
- Hojman’s mechanism becomes especially relevant: local output cannot be read as a simple positive function of local profitability.
- The Chilean question becomes inseparable from multinational allocation and the politics of appropriation.
