# US Militarized Fordism — Hub

## Purpose
This pack reconstructs the **US pole** of the Chile–US relational architecture as a **militarized Fordist / Cold War capitalist formation** rather than as a self-contained national history.

Its job is to specify how military spending, aggregate demand management, labor-market tightening, profitability pressures, inflation politics, and strategic-material governance articulate the US side of the broader project.

## What this pack is for
- Deepen the US side of American / North Atlantic Fordism
- Clarify the macro-political role of military spending in postwar US capitalism
- Build a controlled bridge from US military demand to strategic materials and the copper corridor
- Preserve a reusable mechanism layer for later chapter writing

## What this pack is not
- Not a standalone dissertation on the United States
- Not a replacement for the copper relational cluster
- Not a substitute for the methodological core now being developed in parallel
- Not a license to import a fixed or naturalized concept of “full employment”

## Governing interpretation
This pack treats postwar US capitalism as a historically specific formation in which military spending became a politically admissible form of macroeconomic stabilization and strategic statecraft.

The pack therefore reads:
- military demand as a macroeconomic force
- labor-market tightening as a politically consequential effect
- inflation discourse as partly a distributional and class-conflict language
- strategic materials as a material corridor linking US state power and Chilean copper

## Hard guardrails
- Route all cross-cluster articulation through [[USMF_05_Relation_to_Copper_Cluster]]
- Treat “full employment” as historically descriptive shorthand, not as a fixed theoretical benchmark
- Keep the pack relational: US history here matters because it clarifies the Chile–US connection
- Keep methodological overreach frozen until the research-design package is stabilized
