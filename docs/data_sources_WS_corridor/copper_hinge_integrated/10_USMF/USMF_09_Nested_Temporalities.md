# US Militarized Fordism — Nested Temporalities

## Purpose
This note differentiates temporal scales inside the pack.

Its job is to prevent the conflation of:
- conjuncture
- cycle
- regime
- transition

Barker's account is especially useful here because it moves from specific war booms and recessions to a broader historical argument about "Cold War capitalism" as a distinct political economy, while BPZ isolate the Vietnam years as a particularly revealing moment of labor-market tightening and distributive conflict rather than a whole regime in themselves. See [[USMF_02_Barker2022_Positioning]] and [[USMF_03_BakerPollinZahrt1996_Positioning]].

## Use rule
Every empirical claim used by this pack should be assignable to one of the temporal levels below.

When in doubt, classify the claim first, interpret it second.

## Temporal levels

### Level 1 — Conjuncture
- **Definition:** short-run episode of acceleration, slowdown, or stress
- **Typical duration:** months to a few years
- **Typical objects:** demand shock, labor-market tightening, recession, inflation burst, procurement surge
- **Interpretive question:** what immediate macro-political pressures are active?
- **Examples in this pack:** Korean War boom; Vietnam boom; 1979–1982 recessionary stress; early Reagan defense burst
- **Use in writing:** describe immediate outcomes without overgeneralizing to the regime level

### Level 2 — Political-economic cycle
- **Definition:** medium-run sequence of buildup, conflict, restraint, and partial restabilization
- **Typical duration:** several years to roughly a decade
- **Typical objects:** rearmament followed by inflation conflict, retrenchment, unemployment, and renewed stimulus pressures
- **Interpretive question:** how do policy choices and class conflict evolve across linked conjunctures?
- **Examples in this pack:** Korean War boom -> New Look cuts; Vietnam buildup -> 1970s backlash; Reagan rearmament -> late Cold War reordering
- **Use in writing:** track how militarized demand and backlash interact over time

### Level 3 — Regime
- **Definition:** longer historical organization of accumulation, state form, legitimacy, and macro management
- **Typical duration:** decades
- **Typical objects:** Cold War capitalism; US militarized Fordism; high military Keynesianism as a durable mode of stabilization
- **Interpretive question:** what wider order gives coherence to the cycles?
- **Examples in this pack:** the post-1945 US order in which military spending becomes a central stabilizing and legitimating force
- **Use in writing:** define the historical architecture within which cycles unfold

### Level 4 — Transition between regimes
- **Definition:** historical mutation in which the old regime loses coherence and a new organization emerges without immediate closure
- **Typical duration:** uneven, often spanning several conjunctures and cycles
- **Typical objects:** crisis of high military Keynesianism; transition toward neoliberalism; post-Cold War mutation of state support
- **Interpretive question:** what changes when the old stabilizing formula no longer reproduces itself cleanly?
- **Examples in this pack:** late 1960s through the 1980s; Cold War to post-Cold War mutation
- **Use in writing:** identify structural reorganization without flattening it into one event

## Practical scale rules

### Rule 1 — Vietnam is not the regime
Vietnam is best treated as a conjunctural boom and as part of a wider cycle of expansion, backlash, and retrenchment. It should not stand in for Cold War capitalism as a whole.

### Rule 2 — Reagan is not just another boom
Reagan rearmament has conjunctural demand effects, but it also belongs to a transition in which Cold War militarization helps stabilize a new neoliberal order rather than simply restoring the old one.

### Rule 3 — Cold War capitalism is the regime frame
The regime level names the durable historical architecture in which military spending helps organize macro stabilization, class conflict, and state legitimacy.

### Rule 4 — Copper articulation happens through scale selection
When linking to the copper cluster, first decide whether the claim concerns:
- a conjunctural procurement or stockpiling episode,
- a medium-run strategic-material cycle,
- or a regime-level relation between US militarized accumulation and Chilean copper.

## Scale discipline

### What not to do
- do not call every boom a regime
- do not treat every recession as a regime crisis
- do not conflate Vietnam with Cold War capitalism as a whole
- do not collapse short-run labor-market tightening into a transhistorical theory of "full employment"

### What to do
- specify whether the claim concerns a short-run demand episode, a medium-run policy cycle, a regime form, or a regime transition
- route the Vietnam case through [[USMF_08_Mechanism_Cascade_Notebook]] when the issue is causal sequence
- route broad historical characterization through [[USMF_07_Cycle_and_Transition_Map]] when the issue is periodization
