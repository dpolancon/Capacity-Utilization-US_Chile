# US Militarized Fordism — Cycle and Transition Map

## Purpose
This is the master motion note for the pack.

Its job is to periodize the major phases of US militarized Fordism and identify the transition pressures that move the system from one phase to another.

## Use rule
This note governs **chronology and phase breaks**.
It should stay schematic and phase-oriented rather than becoming a narrative essay.

## Phase map

### Phase 1 — Korean War rearmament boom
- **Approximate window:** 1950–1953
- **Main driver:** abrupt rearmament and elevated defense demand
- **Macroeconomic effect:** strong expansion, output acceleration, restored high-pressure demand
- **Labor-market effect:** sharp tightening of slack; labor gains become politically visible
- **Distributional effect:** early signs of wage pressure and full-employment conflict
- **Strategic-material implication:** direct relevance of strategic procurement and stockpiling
- **Main contradiction activated:** demand stabilization vs inflation control
- **Transition trigger:** post-war retrenchment pressures and anti-inflation politics

### Phase 2 — New Look retrenchment and managed slowdown
- **Approximate window:** 1953–1960
- **Main driver:** effort to reduce fiscal/military burden while preserving Cold War posture
- **Macroeconomic effect:** boom punctuated by recurrent recession and stagnation fears
- **Labor-market effect:** weaker tightness; management recovers leverage
- **Distributional effect:** backlash against prior labor gains
- **Strategic-material implication:** strategic-state role remains, but with tighter fiscal discipline
- **Main contradiction activated:** military necessity vs anti-statist / austerity pressures
- **Transition trigger:** renewed geopolitical and growth anxieties

### Phase 3 — Kennedy/Vietnam expansion
- **Approximate window:** 1961–1969
- **Main driver:** renewed military buildup culminating in Vietnam escalation
- **Macroeconomic effect:** high-growth demand regime; near-peak labor-market absorption
- **Labor-market effect:** exceptionally tight labor markets, broad hiring, stronger worker leverage
- **Distributional effect:** rising wage share and sharper class/distribution conflict
- **Strategic-material implication:** material inputs and global power projection reinforce one another
- **Main contradiction activated:** labor-market tightening vs profit discipline
- **Transition trigger:** inflation politics, profitability concerns, erosion of elite tolerance for high-pressure demand

### Phase 4 — 1970s crisis and retrenchment
- **Approximate window:** 1969–1979
- **Main driver:** declining defense share, inflation crisis, profitability pressure, policy disorientation
- **Macroeconomic effect:** recession, stagnation, unstable recovery pattern
- **Labor-market effect:** rollback of prior tightness; weaker worker bargaining position
- **Distributional effect:** class offensive intensifies under anti-inflation rhetoric
- **Strategic-material implication:** strategic-state logic persists, but can no longer organize the whole regime smoothly
- **Main contradiction activated:** demand management vs inflation / legitimacy crisis
- **Transition trigger:** search for a new politically viable accumulation formula

### Phase 5 — Reagan rearmament and neoliberal transition
- **Approximate window:** 1980–1989
- **Main driver:** renewed defense boom under neoliberal restructuring
- **Macroeconomic effect:** selective re-stimulation without restoring the older regime in full
- **Labor-market effect:** weaker unions and stronger management power limit egalitarian spillovers
- **Distributional effect:** demand support coexists with anti-labor restructuring
- **Strategic-material implication:** Cold War priorities remain central, but now nested in neoliberal transition
- **Main contradiction activated:** militarized stimulus vs anti-state ideology
- **Transition trigger:** waning Soviet threat and mutation of state support mechanisms

### Phase 6 — Post-Cold War mutation
- **Approximate window:** 1990 onward
- **Main driver:** end of Cold War baseline, new forms of stabilization beyond classic military Keynesianism
- **Macroeconomic effect:** military share falls without solving instability of private investment
- **Labor-market effect:** no return to classic high-pressure labor-market politics
- **Distributional effect:** new mechanisms of inequality and demand management emerge
- **Strategic-material implication:** Cold War strategic-material logic mutates rather than disappears
- **Main contradiction activated:** private instability vs new state-support forms
- **Transition trigger:** open for later extension

## Transition logic
### Recurring triggers
- defense expansion or retrenchment
- profitability pressure
- inflation discourse
- labor conflict
- strategic-state reorientation
- geopolitical escalation or de-escalation
