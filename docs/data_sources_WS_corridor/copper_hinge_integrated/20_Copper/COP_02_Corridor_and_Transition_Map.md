# Copper Relational Cluster — Corridor and Transition Map

## Purpose
This is the master motion note for the copper side.
Its job is to periodize the Chile–US copper relation as a moving corridor rather than a static commodity field.

## Corridor logic
U.S. industrial/refining centrality → strategic-state interest → stockpiling/procurement → multinational control and allocation → Chilean appropriation struggles → political conflict over accumulation and sovereignty.

## Phases

### Phase 1 — U.S. capacity formation and corridor prehistory
- Approximate range: late nineteenth century to early twentieth century
- Defining motion: U.S. industrial/refining capacity is built and made internationally extensible.
- Main mechanisms: geology, smelting/refining bottlenecks, trade intermediation, early overseas sourcing.
- Main contradiction: domestic capacity building vs dependence on external ore/material channels.
- Main source anchors: [[Pettengill1935__US_Foreign_Trade_in_Copper]], [[Young1983__Origins_of_the_American_Copper_Industry]]

### Phase 2 — U.S. hub centrality and Chilean insertion
- Approximate range: early twentieth century to World War II
- Defining motion: U.S. refining/trade intermediation and American-controlled mining capital in South America consolidate the corridor.
- Main mechanisms: refining, treatment, merchanting, export orientation, foreign control of production.
- Main contradiction: foreign-controlled extraction vs national appropriation and development.
- Main source anchors: [[Pettengill1935__US_Foreign_Trade_in_Copper]], [[Mamalakis1967__American_Copper_Companies_and_the_Chilean_Government]]

### Phase 3 — Cold War strategic-material consolidation
- Approximate range: 1940s–1950s
- Defining motion: copper is enrolled more explicitly into strategic-state logic through stockpiling and defense planning.
- Main mechanisms: stockpiling, defense procurement, strategic-material classification, Cold War planning.
- Main contradiction: market allocation vs strategic-state intervention.
- Main source anchors: [[Narayan1967__US_Stockpile_Policy_and_the_International_Copper_Industry]], [[USMF_05_Relation_to_Copper_Cluster]]

### Phase 4 — Developmental appropriation struggle
- Approximate range: 1950s–1960s
- Defining motion: Chilean state efforts to retain more copper-derived surplus intensify while the U.S. side continues to rely on the corridor.
- Main mechanisms: taxation, foreign exchange, investment disputes, refinement location, sovereignty claims.
- Main contradiction: national-developmental appropriation vs multinational control of strategic accumulation.
- Main source anchors: [[Mamalakis1967__American_Copper_Companies_and_the_Chilean_Government]]

### Phase 5 — Political rupture and multinational conflict
- Approximate range: late 1960s–early 1970s
- Defining motion: conflict moves from distributive dispute to overt political rupture.
- Main mechanisms: host-state pressure, multinational allocation strategies, supply responses not reducible to local profitability.
- Main contradiction: national sovereignty vs globally organized corporate allocation.
- Main source anchors: [[Hojman1982__Perverse_Supply_Response]]

### Phase 6 — Reconfigured corridor after rupture
- Approximate range: post-1973 onward
- Defining motion: the relation is reconfigured rather than abolished.
- Main mechanisms: new ownership forms, altered appropriation channels, changed strategic-state context, persistence of world-market mediation.
- Main contradiction: apparent national control vs continued insertion into external circuits.
- Main source anchors: [[Rivas_Critical_Use]], [[Shaikh_Basu_Measurement_Constitution]]

## Transition triggers
- Shift in U.S. strategic-state priorities
- Reorganization of refining/trade centrality
- Chilean fiscal/appropriation pressure
- Multinational response and allocation behavior
- Broader regime shifts in U.S. and world capitalism
