# US Militarized Fordism — Core Mechanisms

## Purpose
This note records the recurring mechanism set authorized inside the pack.

Its job is to keep the pack analytically reusable and to prevent drift into pure chronology.

## Use rule
This is the official mechanism register.

The dynamic notes should derive from this register, not invent new mechanism language ad hoc.

## Mechanism set

### M1 — Militarized demand as macro stimulus
A rise in military spending increases effective demand and can stabilize or accelerate output and employment under conditions of slack.

### M2 — Labor-market tightening under high-pressure demand
When militarized demand expands in a setting with underused resources, labor-market slack narrows and bargaining conditions improve for workers.

### M3 — Distributional shift and class conflict
Tighter labor markets can improve wages, employment access, and labor’s bargaining position, thereby intensifying conflict over distribution and control.

### M4 — Inflation discourse as distributional politics
Inflation is not treated here as a purely technical outcome.
It also functions as a political language through which elites contest tight labor markets and wage pressure.

### M5 — Retrenchment and restored discipline
When militarized demand is reduced, or when anti-inflation politics gains hegemony, labor-market slack widens and management discipline is restored.

### M6 — Political admissibility of military spending
Military spending occupies a politically special place because it has historically been more acceptable to dominant groups than equivalent civilian spending aimed at employment or welfare.

### M7 — Strategic materials as material corridor
Militarized demand does not remain abstract.
It runs through concrete procurement systems, stockpiles, industrial inputs, and strategic materials such as copper.

### M8 — Militarized demand as transition mechanism
In some conjunctures, military spending does not simply repeat the old regime but helps manage the transition into a new one.
