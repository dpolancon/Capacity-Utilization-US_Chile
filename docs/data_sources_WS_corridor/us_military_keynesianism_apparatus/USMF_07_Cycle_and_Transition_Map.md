# US Militarized Fordism — Cycle and Transition Map

## Purpose
This is the master motion note for the pack.

Its job is to periodize the major phases of US militarized Fordism and identify the transition pressures that move the system from one phase to another.

## Use rule
This note governs **chronology and phase breaks**.
It should stay schematic and phase-oriented rather than becoming a narrative essay.

## High-level proposition
Cold War capitalism should be read as a historically specific regime in which military spending helped set the tempo of macroeconomic life, but did so through alternating booms, retrenchments, and redistributive conflicts rather than through stable consensus. Read [[USMF_02_Barker2022_Positioning]] for the long-run architecture and [[USMF_03_BakerPollinZahrt1996_Positioning]] for the Vietnam-era tightening mechanism.

## Phase map

### Phase 1 — Korean War rearmament boom
- **Approximate window:** 1950–1953
- **Main driver:** abrupt rearmament and elevated defense demand
- **Macroeconomic effect:** strong expansion; initial inflation burst was contained by controls
- **Labor-market effect:** unusually tight labor markets; labor gained bargaining leverage
- **Distributional effect:** early full-employment profit squeeze, though partly mitigated by controls and postwar institutional settlement
- **Strategic-material implication:** direct relevance of procurement, stockpiling, and strategic materials administration
- **Why this phase matters:** this is the first decisive demonstration that very large defense budgets could sustain high-pressure demand without destroying capitalism
- **Transition trigger:** retrenchment pressures after the boom, anti-inflation politics, and search for a cheaper Cold War posture
- **Primary source basis:** Barker's political business cycle schema and discussion of rearmament as the breakthrough that overcame prior resistance to huge defense budgets

### Phase 2 — New Look retrenchment and managed slowdown
- **Approximate window:** 1953–1961
- **Main driver:** effort to reduce the fiscal burden of high military Keynesianism while preserving strategic superiority
- **Macroeconomic effect:** short boom followed by recurrent recessions and stagnation fears
- **Labor-market effect:** weakening of prior tightness; management recovers discipline over labor
- **Distributional effect:** management offensive strengthens, especially after mid-1950s erosion of earlier labor settlement
- **Strategic-material implication:** strategic-state functions persist, but under tighter budget discipline and greater concern with inflation and the dollar
- **Why this phase matters:** demonstrates that defense retrenchment can reopen macro instability even when military capacity remains historically high
- **Transition trigger:** renewed geopolitical anxiety plus concern that slower growth threatened US power and competitiveness
- **Primary source basis:** Barker's New Look phase in the political business cycle and his broader argument that retrenchment repeatedly fed recessionary pressure

### Phase 3 — Kennedy/Vietnam expansion
- **Approximate window:** 1961–1969
- **Main driver:** renewed buildup followed by Vietnam escalation
- **Macroeconomic effect:** slow expansion with price stability gives way to rapid expansion with rising inflation
- **Labor-market effect:** exceptionally tight labor markets, wider labor-force absorption, broader hiring of previously excluded workers
- **Distributional effect:** rising wage share, poverty reduction, stronger bargaining power for labor, and sharper conflict over profits and inflation
- **Strategic-material implication:** military demand, global power projection, and material inputs reinforce one another inside a still-functioning Cold War order
- **Why this phase matters:** this is the clearest late-Fordist case of militarized demand acting as a politically admissible route to labor-market tightening and egalitarian gains
- **Transition trigger:** rising inflation politics, profitability concerns, and breakdown of elite tolerance for a high-pressure labor market
- **Primary source basis:** Barker on the Vietnam boom as a central high military Keynesian phase; BPZ on war spending, labor-market tightening, distributive gains, and backlash
- **Guardrail:** BPZ's “full employment” language must be re-read through [[USMF_06_Guardrails]] as historically contingent labor-market tightening rather than a natural or fixed benchmark

### Phase 4 — 1970s crisis and retrenchment
- **Approximate window:** 1969–1979
- **Main driver:** reduction in real defense spending, inflation crisis, profitability pressure, and policy disorientation
- **Macroeconomic effect:** recession, stagnation, unstable recovery, and no coherent replacement for the earlier demand regime
- **Labor-market effect:** rollback of prior tightness; weakening of labor's relative bargaining power
- **Distributional effect:** management offensive intensifies under anti-inflation rhetoric; social spending expands in some domains but without restoring prior labor strength
- **Strategic-material implication:** strategic-state logic continues, but no longer organizes the whole regime smoothly or consensually
- **Why this phase matters:** this is the crisis of high military Keynesianism, where the disruptive effects of militarized demand begin to outweigh the stabilizing effects
- **Transition trigger:** search for a new accumulation formula able to support profits, discipline labor, and retain politically acceptable state activism
- **Primary source basis:** Barker's claim that the age of high military Keynesianism fell into crisis during and after the Vietnam boom and that the 1970s lacked a viable replacement formula

### Phase 5 — Reagan rearmament and neoliberal transition
- **Approximate window:** 1977–1989
- **Main driver:** Second Cold War rearmament nested inside neoliberal restructuring
- **Macroeconomic effect:** recession and high unemployment early in the period followed by selective restimulation; price stability restored on new terms
- **Labor-market effect:** weakened unions and stronger management power prevent the egalitarian spillovers associated with earlier tight labor markets
- **Distributional effect:** militarized demand coexists with anti-labor restructuring, austerity in social policy, and pro-elite tax reform
- **Strategic-material implication:** Cold War priorities remain central, but increasingly as support for a broader neoliberal order rather than as the whole regime logic
- **Why this phase matters:** demonstrates that militarized demand survives the old order and helps midwife the new one, even while ceasing to define the entire system
- **Transition trigger:** waning Soviet threat and mutation of state-support mechanisms away from classic Cold War forms
- **Primary source basis:** Barker on Reagan as both a military Keynesian and a transition figure whose defense boom helped secure otherwise difficult neoliberal measures

### Phase 6 — Post-Cold War mutation
- **Approximate window:** 1990 onward
- **Main driver:** decline of the Cold War baseline and rise of new forms of stabilization beyond classic defense-led demand management
- **Macroeconomic effect:** defense share falls sharply without solving the instability of private investment
- **Labor-market effect:** no return to classic high-pressure labor-market politics of the Korea/Vietnam type
- **Distributional effect:** new inequality mechanisms and new forms of state support emerge
- **Strategic-material implication:** older strategic-material logics mutate rather than vanish, often routed through different security and financial forms
- **Why this phase matters:** marks the afterlife of Cold War capitalism rather than a clean break from it
- **Transition trigger:** open for later extension
- **Primary source basis:** Barker's epilogue logic from military Keynesianism to the asset economy and central-bank-centered stabilization

## Transition logic
### Recurring triggers
- defense expansion or retrenchment
- profitability pressure
- inflation discourse
- labor conflict
- strategic-state reorientation
- geopolitical escalation or de-escalation

### Compact formula
Rearmament can temporarily stabilize demand, but once labor-market tightening and inflation politics sharpen distributional conflict, elites seek retrenchment or restructuring; renewed insecurity then reopens the political space for militarized demand.

## Controlled language
- Prefer: **tight labor-market conditions**, **high-pressure demand**, **labor-market absorption**, **militarized demand**
- Avoid as master categories: **natural rate of unemployment**, **fixed full-employment benchmark**
