# US Militarized Fordism — Dynamic Contradiction Matrix

## Purpose
This note tracks the contradictions activated, displaced, or intensified across the phases of US militarized Fordism.

Its job is diagnostic rather than narrative.

## Use rule
Keep prose minimal.
Use the matrix to compare how contradictions move across phases.

## Contradiction set

### C1 — Demand stabilization vs private control of investment
The state needs to stabilize accumulation, but capitalism preserves private command over investment.

### C2 — Labor-market tightening vs profit discipline
Tight labor markets strengthen workers while threatening elite preferences for discipline and profitability.

### C3 — Military spending vs inflation control
Defense outlays can stabilize demand while also intensifying inflation politics.

### C4 — Strategic-state expansion vs anti-statist ideology
The state expands materially while ideology continues to police state action.

### C5 — Defense legitimacy vs welfare limits
Large military budgets are politically easier to authorize than comparably ambitious civilian spending.

### C6 — Global hegemony vs domestic distributive stability
External power projection can destabilize domestic distributive compromise.

## Matrix

| Phase | C1 | C2 | C3 | C4 | C5 | C6 | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Korean War boom | intensified | intensified | intensified | intensified | stabilized | intensified | Rearmament solved the demand problem without socializing investment, but generated inflation pressure and a visible labor/profit squeeze. |
| New Look retrenchment | intensified | displaced | stabilized | intensified | stabilized | present | Budget cuts and anti-inflation discipline reduced tight-labor conflict, but reopened instability and preserved the asymmetry between defense legitimacy and civilian limits. |
| Kennedy/Vietnam expansion | intensified | intensified | intensified | intensified | stabilized | intensified | This is the sharpest phase of militarized labor-market tightening: broader hiring, rising wage share, and stronger backlash over inflation and profitability. |
| 1970s crisis/retrenchment | intensified | displaced | intensified | intensified | destabilized | intensified | High military Keynesianism loses coherence; distribution conflict is re-coded as inflation crisis, while no replacement regime yet stabilizes growth. |
| Reagan rearmament | stabilized | displaced | present | intensified | stabilized | intensified | Militarized demand returns, but now under anti-labor neoliberal conditions; defense remains legitimate while welfare is disciplined. |
| Post-Cold War mutation | open | displaced | open | intensified | destabilized | open | Classic Cold War form weakens, but state support for accumulation mutates rather than disappears. |

## Short interpretive comments

### Korean War boom
- C1 intensifies because demand support is state-led while control over investment remains private.
- C2 and C3 intensify because tight labor markets and inflation fears emerge together.
- C5 is stabilized because military spending remains more politically acceptable than civilian socialization.

### New Look retrenchment
- C2 is displaced, not resolved: labor discipline returns, but through a weaker demand environment.
- C1 remains active because retrenchment reopens the underlying instability that militarized demand had masked.
- C4 remains intense because anti-statist ideology coexists with a still-heavy strategic state.

### Kennedy/Vietnam expansion
- C2 reaches maximum force: labor-market tightening becomes broad and politically consequential.
- C3 intensifies as inflation discourse becomes the preferred way to attack a distributional shift unfavorable to capital.
- C6 intensifies because external military projection increasingly destabilizes domestic compromise.

### 1970s crisis/retrenchment
- C3 and C6 dominate the phase.
- C5 becomes destabilized because defense no longer automatically secures legitimacy while social spending expands unevenly.
- This is the phase where the old demand fix no longer yields an easily governable political economy.

### Reagan rearmament
- C1 is temporarily stabilized by renewed defense demand plus a restored pro-profit policy environment.
- C2 is displaced because labor has been institutionally weakened; demand no longer produces the same egalitarian spillovers.
- C4 becomes especially sharp because anti-state rhetoric now coexists with massive state support through defense.

### Post-Cold War mutation
- Several contradictions remain open because the classic Cold War form disappears, but the problem of unstable private investment remains.
- C4 persists because new state-support mechanisms continue under market-centered ideology.

## Reading guide
- **intensified** = contradiction becomes politically central
- **displaced** = contradiction is suppressed or shifted temporarily
- **stabilized** = contradiction is managed without being resolved
- **destabilized** = prior mode of management loses coherence
- **present** = contradiction remains active but not dominant
- **open** = requires later extension beyond current pack

## Controlled translation rule
Where BPZ speaks of “full employment,” read the contradiction primarily as one of **tight labor-market conditions vs profit discipline**, not as deviation from or return to a natural employment benchmark. See [[USMF_06_Guardrails]].
