# Barker 2022 — Positioning

## Full reference
Tim Barker, _Cold War Capitalism: The Political Economy of American Military Spending, 1947–1990_.

## Why this source matters
This is the pack's main long-run US-side deepener.

Its central value is that it reconstructs postwar US capitalism as a historically specific formation in which military spending was not peripheral, but a constitutive macro-political force shaping growth, recession, employment, and transition.

## Main object
- American military spending, 1947–1990
- the macroeconomic role of defense demand
- the relation between Cold War statecraft and capitalist stabilization
- the transition from high military Keynesianism to neoliberalism

## Core contribution to this pack
Barker provides the **phase architecture** for US militarized Fordism.

Use this source to anchor:
- periodization of the US-side cycle
- the distinction between Korean boom, New Look retrenchment, Vietnam expansion, 1970s crisis, and Reagan rearmament
- the claim that military spending helped set the tempo of postwar US macroeconomic life

## Best use
- governing source for [[USMF_07_Cycle_and_Transition_Map]]
- principal anchor for [[USMF_09_Nested_Temporalities]]
- major input for contradiction tracking in [[USMF_10_Dynamic_Contradiction_Matrix]]

## Main mechanism claims to extract
- military spending as a politically admissible form of macroeconomic stabilization
- defense retrenchment as a contributor to recessionary pressure
- military booms as moments of stronger labor demand and altered class conflict
- Reagan rearmament as a transition mechanism rather than a simple repetition of earlier Cold War patterns

## Why it complements Vidal
Vidal supplies regime grammar.
Barker supplies a richer empirical account of the US pole of that regime.

So the relation is:
- Vidal = what American / North Atlantic Fordism is
- Barker = how the US side was cyclically stabilized, stressed, and transformed

## Why it complements the copper cluster
This source helps explain why strategic materials mattered to the US side at all.

It does not replace copper history.
It clarifies the macro-political architecture into which copper, stockpiling, and strategic procurement were inserted.

## Main gain
This source prevents the US side from appearing as either:
- a vague background of “Fordism,” or
- a purely narrative history of Cold War policy

It gives the pack a usable long-duration motion structure.

## Main risks
- US recentring: it can overwhelm the relational function of the pack if allowed to become the main story
- macro overreach: it can tempt the project into a full US political-economy dissertation

## Use rule
Use Barker as:
- the main phase-architecture source
- a macro-political deepener
- a historically grounded account of military demand

Do **not** use Barker as:
- a replacement for the copper relational cluster
- a replacement for the methodological core
- a license to detach the US side from the Chile–US relation
