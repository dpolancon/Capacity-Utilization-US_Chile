# Branch Summary — ChaoGrid Exploration  
## Q2 vs Q3 Polynomial Basis Across Regime Windows

---

## Scope of This Exploration

This branch implemented and evaluated a Johansen reduced-rank grid search across:

- **Windows**
  - Full: 1925–2023  
  - Fordism: 1925–1973  
  - Post-Fordism: 1974–2023  

- **Deterministics**
  - `ecdet ∈ {none, const}`  
  - Trend excluded by design  

- **Polynomial Basis**
  - Q2: Quadratic specification  
  - Q3: Cubic specification  

- **Lag Structure**
  - Grid over `p`
  - Johansen executed with `K = p + 1`

- **Model Selection Criteria**
  - PIC
  - BIC

A structured visualization pack (S5.1–S5.7) was produced to support systematic comparison.

---

## Core Empirical Findings

### 1. Dominant Structure: Minimal Dynamics and Low Rank

Across windows and bases:

- PIC-optimal specification is overwhelmingly:
  - `p = 1`
  - `r = 1`
  - `ecdet = none`

- BIC consistently prefers:
  - `p = 1`
  - `r = 0`

**Implication:**  
The system is empirically consistent with a low-dimensional long-run structure and minimal short-run dynamics.

There is no systematic evidence that higher lag orders are required in either regime.

---

### 2. Q2 vs Q3: No Robust Regime Differentiation

Key observations:

- Q3 does not produce meaningful PIC improvements in Post-Fordism.
- ΔPIC differences between Q2 and Q3 are very small (typically < 0.1).
- Rank and lag choices remain essentially unchanged when moving from Q2 to Q3.

**Conclusion:**  
There is no robust econometric evidence that Post-Fordism requires cubic polynomial structure.

Quadratic structure appears sufficient.

---

### 3. Boundary-Seeking Minima Explain Q3 “Anomalies”

The only dramatic Q3 case occurs in:

- Fordism × No Constant
- (p = 5, r = 4)

However:

- This is a boundary-seeking minimum (max p, max r).
- It lies at the edge of the admissible region.
- It is explicitly flagged by the boundary diagnostics.

**By design principle:**  
Boundary-seeking minima are treated as overparameterization signals, not structural findings.

Thus, the only strong Q3 deviation is classified as overfitting.

---

### 4. Rank Stability Across Regimes

Within interior solutions:

- `r = 1` dominates across full, Ford, and Post windows.
- There is no systematic increase in rank in Post-Fordism.

**Interpretation:**  
The dimensionality of the long-run manifold appears stable across regimes.

Regime differentiation does not manifest through changes in reduced-rank dimensionality.

---

### 5. Weak Separation Between Competing Models

Across nearly all windows:

- ΔPIC between top candidates is small.
- Multiple specifications are statistically close.

This implies:

- Model choice is fragile at the criterion level.
- Parsimony and stability diagnostics should guide final selection.
- Criterion-based claims about polynomial order are not strongly identified.

---

## What Can Be Concluded

From this exploration:

1. A low-dimensional reduced-rank structure (r ≈ 1) with minimal lag length (p = 1) is broadly supported.
2. Increasing polynomial flexibility from Q2 to Q3 does not robustly change dimensionality or lag selection.
3. Apparent Q3 gains are boundary-driven artifacts.
4. Regime differentiation, if present, is unlikely to operate through polynomial order or rank expansion.

---

## What Cannot Be Concluded

This branch does **not** support claims that:

- Post-Fordism requires cubic structure.
- Fordism is “quadratic” while Post-Fordism is “cubic.”
- Cointegration rank changes across regimes.
- Q3 is inferentially superior.

Additionally:

- Q2 and Q3 currently differ in system dimension (`m = 4` vs `m = 5`).
- Rank-decision exports require repair before inferential use.
- θ(e) inference module is not yet operational.

---

## Theoretical Implication

The findings are consistent with:

- Drift in individual variables (I(1) behavior),
- Stability emerging along restricted long-run subspaces,
- Regime changes operating through parameter shifts within a stable manifold,
  rather than through expansion of its dimensionality.

If regimes differ, they likely do so through:

- Parameter drift (β changes),
- Adjustment dynamics (α changes),
- Structural breaks,
- Variance structure.

Not through higher polynomial order.

---

## Next Steps (Deferred)

Future development should:

1. Enforce polynomial comparability under fixed system dimension.
2. Repair rank-decision exports.
3. Implement parameter stability diagnostics.
4. Test stability of the cointegration space across windows.
5. Introduce disciplined tie-breakers:
   - Residual diagnostics
   - Recursive estimation
   - Rolling forecast performance

---

## Bottom Line

This exploration does **not** provide evidence that Post-Fordism requires higher-order nonlinear structure relative to Fordism.

Instead, it supports:

- Low-dimensional reduced-rank structure,
- Minimal lag dynamics,
- Stable long-run dimensionality across regimes.

Regime differentiation must be investigated through parameter stability and adjustment structure, not polynomial expansion.
