4.X Stage 3 — Dynamic Admissibility Under Full Sample and Regime
Partition

Stage 1 established that the confinement dimension of the system is
invariant across historical windows. The reduced-rank lattice
consistently selects r = 1, indicating that regime transformation does
not operate through a change in manifold dimensionality.

Stage 2 demonstrated that regime differences instead appear in the
geometry of feasibility. The admissible (p,r) domain is compressed in
the Fordist subsample, wider in the full sample, and structurally
differentiated in the post-Fordist window. Rank remains constant; domain
width varies.

Stage 3 addresses a more restrictive question:

Which level specifications consistent with the confinement architecture
are dynamically admissible?

This stage does not perform inference, nor does it test parameter
stability. It evaluates whether the level representation survives
asymptotic aggregation and minimal dynamic discipline. Admissibility is
treated as a necessary precondition for long-run interpretation.

4.X.1 Admissibility Framework

Admissibility is defined through a double gate.

Gate 1 — Asymptotic Aggregation. We implement the Bayer–Hanck combined
cointegration framework, which aggregates Engle–Granger, Johansen,
Boswijk, and Banerjee–Dolado–Mestre statistics into a unified
Fisher-type measure. The combined statistic serves as an asymptotic
screen for level relations without privileging a single testing
approach.

Gate 2 — Dynamic Bounds Discipline. We apply ARDL bounds testing under
strict lag discipline p = 1 for the dependent variable and q = 1 for all
regressors. This restriction is intentional. It prevents dynamic
overfitting from artificially stabilizing weak level relations. The
bounds F-statistic governs admissibility; only specifications rejecting
the null of no level relationship pass the dynamic gate.

A specification is classified as Stage 3 admissible only if it passes
both gates.

4.X.2 Full Sample Results

Consistent with the research prior, we begin with the pooled sample
before turning to regime partition.

Under the full sample, admissibility is narrow. Only the cubic
specification (Q3) with a constant term survives the double gate.
Quadratic specifications and deterministic-free variants fail either the
asymptotic aggregation or the dynamic bounds discipline.

Two baseline facts follow.

First, a level relation is dynamically feasible in pooled data.

Second, that feasibility is specification-sensitive and relies on
deterministic anchoring.

The pooled sample does not generate universal admissibility. Even before
partitioning, the dynamically feasible domain is constrained.

4.X.3 Regime Partition: Fordism and Post-Fordism

We now compare the pooled evidence to the regime partition, testing the
regulationist prior that historical regimes mediate accumulation
dynamics.

Fordism (1925–1973)

Under Fordism, admissibility expands relative to the pooled sample. Both
quadratic (Q2) and cubic (Q3) specifications with a constant pass the
double gate, under both raw and orthogonal interaction bases.

This result is consistent with Stage 2’s feasibility geometry. The
Fordist subsample exhibited a tightly structured admissible lattice.
Stage 3 shows that within that compressed geometry, the level relation
remains dynamically coherent under minimal discipline.

Fordism therefore combines: - Invariant confinement dimension, -
Compressed but internally stable feasibility domain, - Robust dynamic
admissibility under strict lag discipline.

The interaction block and capital accumulation operate within a stable
dynamic envelope.

Post-Fordism (1974–2023)

The post-Fordist window yields a sharply different pattern.

Under strict discipline, no specification passes the double gate. While
certain single-test components—most notably Johansen—remain significant,
the Bayer–Hanck aggregation fails, and the ARDL bounds test does not
support a stable level relation at p = 1, q = 1.

This outcome must be interpreted carefully.

First, it does not indicate a change in confinement dimension. Stage 1
established that r = 1 is invariant across windows. The structural
manifold remains one-dimensional.

Second, it does not imply absence of interaction dynamics. Stage 2
demonstrated that the feasibility geometry remains well-defined.

What it reveals is a contraction of the dynamically admissible domain
under minimal discipline. The level specification cannot survive strict
lag restriction in the post-Fordist regime.

To assess whether this contraction is purely mechanical, we implemented
bounded auto-ARDL selection using BIC. Allowing regressor lag depth to
expand rescues constant specifications in post-Fordism. The dependent
variable lag remains p = 1, but additional lags on capital and
interaction terms restore dynamic admissibility. Deterministic-free
specifications, however, remain non-admissible, and one cubic variant
reaches boundary lag selection, indicating overfitting risk rather than
robust stabilization.

Lag flexibility therefore mitigates but does not eliminate the
contraction. Under the strict admissibility criterion, post-Fordism
exhibits zero admissible specifications.

4.X.4 Variance Regime and Dynamic Fragility

The contraction of admissibility in post-Fordism should not be
interpreted mechanically as structural disappearance of the long-run
relation. Stage 1 diagnostics documented heavy tails, regime-like
persistence, and heteroskedastic behavior in level variables. It is
therefore plausible that post-Fordism exhibits higher conditional
variance and variance instability relative to Fordism.

Under such conditions, asymptotic aggregation and ARDL bounds
testing—particularly under strict lag discipline—become fragile to
variance shifts and clustered volatility. The failure of strict
admissibility in post-Fordism may reflect not a change in confinement
dimension, but an increase in dynamic fragility. The long-run relation,
if present, requires thicker lag buffering or deterministic anchoring to
remain coherent.

Higher variance does not eliminate the structural manifold; it
compresses the minimally disciplined dynamic envelope within which that
manifold can be expressed.

Formal treatment of conditional heteroskedasticity is deferred to the
inference stage, where robust and variance-aware procedures will be
implemented explicitly.

4.X.5 Regulationist Synthesis

Taken together, the results align with the regulationist prior.

-   The confinement dimension remains invariant across regimes.
-   The feasibility geometry differs across regimes.
-   Dynamic admissibility under minimal discipline is
    regime-conditional.

Fordism supports a dynamically coherent long-run envelope. The full
sample supports a narrower admissible set. Post-Fordism exhibits
contraction of the admissible domain and heightened dynamic fragility.

Regime transformation therefore operates not through a change in
structural dimensionality, but through a change in stability margins and
admissible dynamics.

Stage 3 closes the admissibility question. It identifies which
specifications are dynamically coherent candidates for long-run
interpretation within each historical window. It does not establish
parameter constancy or structural stability; those tasks follow in
subsequent stages.

Only specifications passing the strict double gate proceed to the
inference architecture.
