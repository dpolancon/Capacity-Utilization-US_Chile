Stage 2 — Feasibility Geometry and Comparable-Domain Structure
------------------------------------------------------------------

From Full Sample Baseline to Regime-Partition Diagnostics

Stage 2 builds directly on the confinement architecture established in Stage 1. There, the unrestricted lattice search over (p,r) revealed a sharply identified global minimum at (p=1, r=1) across polynomial degrees (Q2, Q3), bases (raw and orthogonal), and deterministic specifications. The long-run confinement dimension proved invariant across historical partitions: the system’s drift is restricted to a one-dimensional cointegrating manifold in the full sample, and this dimensionality does not shift under Fordist or Post-Fordist splits.

Stage 2 does not revisit rank identification. Instead, it interrogates the geometry of admissibility: how feasibility constraints, comparability restrictions, and sample length interact to shape the effective model space across historical regimes.

The organizing prior is explicit: the full historical sample defines the structural baseline; regime partitions are introduced to test the regulationist hypothesis that institutional transformations alter the dynamic margins of the system without necessarily redefining its long-run confinement structure.

1. Full Sample as Structural Benchmark

Across Q2 and Q3 specifications, and under both deterministic settings, the full sample exhibits a broad feasible lattice. In Q2 (system dimension m=5), the feasible lag region extends up to p_max=7. In Q3 (m=6), the feasible bound remains p_max=7. Crucially:

- All computed cells are also comparable cells.
- The comparable-domain restriction does not eliminate any computed specifications.
- The share of computed models is high (approximately 0.80–0.83 across deterministics and bases).
- Gate failures are effectively absent in the full sample windows.

This geometry confirms that the full historical sample provides a wide admissible domain for dynamic specification. The identification of (p=1, r=1) is therefore not the product of a mechanically truncated lattice. It emerges from a broad feasible space in which alternative lag structures are available but not selected by the information criteria.

The full sample thus establishes two structural facts:

(1) The confinement dimension is one.
(2) The admissible dynamic region is wide.

This combination provides the benchmark against which regime partitions must be interpreted.

2. Regime Partition: Feasibility Compression

When the sample is partitioned into Fordism and Post-Fordism, the rank remains invariant at r=1. What changes is not confinement dimension but the geometry of feasibility.

2.1 Fordism

The Fordist window exhibits pronounced feasibility compression:

- In Q2 (m=5), p_max shrinks to 3.
- In Q3 (m=6), p_max tightens further to 2 under constant deterministics.
- Computed shares fall dramatically (approximately 0.24–0.34 under Q3 const).
- Gate failures dominate the lattice in higher-degree specifications.

The Fordist lattice therefore appears as a narrow admissible strip within the full (p,r) grid. Large portions of the potential dynamic space are ruled out by feasibility constraints (rank conditions, sample length, or admissibility gates).

Importantly, however:

- The minimum remains at (p=1, r=1).
- The comparable-domain filter does not exclude any computed cells.
- The selected specification lies strictly within the interior of the feasible region.

Thus, feasibility compression does not induce a rank shift. It tightens the margins within which admissible dynamics can be estimated.

2.2 Post-Fordism

The Post-Fordist window exhibits intermediate geometry:

- In Q2, p_max reaches 3–4 depending on deterministic specification.
- In Q3, p_max is 2–3.
- Computed shares are larger than Fordism but smaller than the full sample.

Feasibility expands relative to Fordism but does not return to the breadth of the full historical sample. Again, the selected minimum remains (p=1, r=1), and comparable-domain filtering does not alter the computed lattice.

3. Comparable-Domain Geometry

A central concern in multi-window model selection is whether cross-regime comparisons are artificially induced by unequal feasible domains. Stage 2 addresses this explicitly.

For Q2 (m=5):
- The cross-window comparable bound is p ≤ 3.

For Q3 (m=6):
- The cross-window comparable bound tightens to p ≤ 2.

These bounds are determined by the short-window regimes (Fordism and Post-Fordism) under constant deterministics. However:

- The unrestricted PIC and BIC minima are always at p=1.
- The comparable-domain restriction does not remove any computed cell.
- The global minimum lies strictly within the comparable region in every window.

Therefore, comparability constraints define the admissible cross-regime intersection but do not drive model selection. The identification of (p=1, r=1) is not a boundary artifact.

4. Structural Interpretation

Combining Stages 1 and 2 yields a precise structural statement.

(i) Confinement dimension is invariant.  
Across full sample, Fordism, and Post-Fordism, the system’s long-run drift is confined to a one-dimensional manifold.

(ii) Admissible domain width varies historically.  
The Fordist regime exhibits severe compression of the feasible lattice; Post-Fordism partially relaxes this constraint; the full sample exhibits the widest admissible region.

(iii) Rank does not mediate regime differentiation.  
Regime transformation does not operate through a change in cointegration rank.

(iv) Institutional mediation manifests through stability margins.  
What varies across regimes is the tolerance of the system to alternative short-run dynamics — the width of the dynamically admissible space.

This result is theoretically consequential. If regimes corresponded to distinct structural equilibria in a strong sense, one would expect rank to shift across partitions. Instead, the dimensionality of confinement remains constant. Regime change appears as variation in admissible dynamic flexibility, not as redefinition of the structural manifold.

In regulationist terms, institutional configurations modulate stability margins rather than altering the underlying long-run structural constraint.

5. Stage 2 Closure

Stage 2 therefore consolidates the empirical architecture:

- The long-run manifold is one-dimensional across all historical partitions.
- The selected dynamic order is parsimonious (p=1).
- Comparability restrictions do not distort identification.
- Regime differences manifest through feasibility compression, not rank shifts.

With the confinement dimension established and the geometry of admissibility clarified, the analysis is now positioned to proceed to structural-break testing and dynamic inference in Stage 3.
