4.X Stage 3 — Double Admissibility Gate in Levels

Stage 3 evaluates whether the level specifications identified in the
reduced-rank lattice are dynamically admissible. The objective is not
inference, parameter stability, or historical interpretation. The
purpose is narrower and prior: to determine whether a given
specification can be treated as a feasible long-run relation under
minimal dynamic discipline.

To that end, admissibility is defined through a double gate.

Gate 1 (Asymptotic Meta-Test). We implement the Bayer–Hanck combined
cointegration framework, which aggregates Engle–Hanck, Johansen,
Boswijk, and Banerjee–Dolado–Mestre tests into a single Fisher-type
statistic. The combined statistic (BH_all) serves as a preliminary
screen for level relations. This gate answers whether, under asymptotic
critical values, the specification exhibits sufficient evidence of
cointegration across heterogeneous testing frameworks. It is not treated
as definitive; it is an admissibility filter.

Gate 2 (Dynamic Bounds Discipline). We then apply ARDL bounds testing to
evaluate whether the level relation survives dynamic specification. In
the strict version of the gate, lag structure is fixed at p = 1 for the
dependent variable and q = 1 for all regressors. This rule is
intentionally conservative. The bounds F-statistic governs
admissibility: only specifications that reject the null of no level
relationship are considered dynamically admissible. The t-bounds
statistic is reported for completeness but does not override the F
decision.

A specification is classified as Stage 3 admissible only if it passes
both gates.

4.X.1 Results Under Strict Dynamic Discipline

Under the strict p = 1, q = 1 discipline, admissibility is strongly
regime-conditional.

In the Fordist period (1925–1973), both quadratic (Q2) and cubic (Q3)
specifications with a constant term pass the double gate, under both raw
and orthogonal interaction bases. This indicates that the long-run level
relation between output, capital, and the interaction block is
dynamically coherent within the Fordist regime.

For the full sample, admissibility narrows: only the constant Q3
specification survives the double gate. This suggests that pooling
across regimes compresses the dynamically feasible domain.

In contrast, no specification passes the strict double gate in the
post-Fordist period (1974–2023). Although certain single-test components
(notably Johansen) remain significant, the combined Bayer–Hanck
statistic fails, and the ARDL bounds test under strict lag discipline
does not support a stable level relation.

Importantly, this outcome does not imply a change in confinement
dimension. Upstream reduced-rank analysis continues to select r = 1
across windows. Rather, Stage 3 reveals a contraction in the admissible
dynamic domain under post-Fordist conditions.

4.X.2 Lag Sensitivity: Auto-ARDL Robustness

To assess whether the post-Fordist failure is purely mechanical—driven
by restrictive lag structure—we implement a bounded auto-ARDL procedure
using BIC-based lag selection.

The results are revealing.

First, in all admissible cases, the selected lag for the dependent
variable remains p = 1. The binding constraint in the strict gate is not
the lag on output growth, but the restriction on regressor lags.

Second, constant specifications in the post-Fordist window become
dynamically admissible once regressor lag structure expands (typically
two lags on capital and one lag on interaction terms). The ARDL
F-statistic then rejects the null of no level relationship.

Third, deterministic-free (no-intercept) specifications remain
non-admissible even under flexible lag selection. In one cubic
specification, the lag search reaches the maximum boundary and produces
undefined F-statistics, indicating overfitting risk rather than
structural support.

These findings clarify the nature of post-Fordist instability. The
failure of strict admissibility is not attributable to insufficient
dynamics on the dependent variable. It reflects sensitivity to
deterministic structure and lag depth on capital and interaction terms.
The long-run relation, if present, requires a thicker dynamic buffer in
the post-Fordist regime.

For purposes of Stage 3 classification, however, the strict gate remains
the operative criterion. The auto-ARDL results are interpreted as
robustness checks, not as replacements for the disciplined admissibility
rule.

4.X.3 Interpretation: Admissibility as Regime-Conditional Feasibility

Stage 3 therefore yields a clear conclusion.

Admissibility of the level specification is regime-conditional. Fordism
supports dynamically coherent long-run relations across polynomial
degrees and basis choices. The full sample supports a narrower
admissible set. Post-Fordism fails the strict admissibility criterion.

Crucially, this regime variation does not operate through a shift in
cointegration rank. The confinement dimension remains invariant. What
varies is the width of the dynamically feasible specification domain. In
Fordism, the interaction block and capital accumulation operate within a
stable dynamic envelope. In Post-Fordism, that envelope contracts: level
relations require either expanded lag structure or deterministic
support, and deterministic-free formulations fail altogether.

Stage 3 thus closes the admissibility question. It does not establish
parameter constancy, structural stability, or historical causation.
Those tasks belong to subsequent stages. What it establishes is narrower
but essential: which specifications are dynamically coherent candidates
for long-run interpretation within each regime.

Only those specifications passing the double gate proceed to Stage 4.
