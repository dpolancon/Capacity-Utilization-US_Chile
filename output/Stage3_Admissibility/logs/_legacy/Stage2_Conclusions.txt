Stage 2 — Feasibility Geometry and Comparable-Domain Discipline

Stage 2 establishes the admissible geometry of the lag–rank lattice
prior to any structural interpretation. Its purpose is not selection,
but delimitation: to identify which regions of the (p,r) space are
statistically feasible within each regime window and to determine
whether cross-window comparability imposes additional restrictions
beyond intrinsic feasibility.

1.  System Dimension and Feasible Lag Boundaries

The polynomial specification determines system dimension. In the
quadratic specification (Q2), the state vector has dimension m=5; in the
cubic specification (Q3), m=6. This increase in dimension mechanically
tightens finite-sample feasibility constraints in shorter subsamples.

The feasible maximum lag p_max_feasible varies systematically across
windows:

-   Full sample (T=99): p_max_feasible = 7 under both deterministics and
    both polynomial degrees.
-   Fordism (T=49) and post-Fordism (T=50):
    -   Q2 (m=5): p_max_feasible in {3,4}.
    -   Q3 (m=6): p_max_feasible in {2,3}, with the most restrictive
        case arising under ecdet = “const”.

The tightening from Q2 to Q3 is therefore fully accounted for by
dimensional expansion, not by regime-specific instability. Short windows
bind earlier because the ratio T / (m p) contracts as m increases.

The first structural result of Stage 2 is therefore:

The admissible region of the lag–rank lattice is window-specific and
contracts as system dimension rises, but this contraction is
mechanically explained by finite-sample feasibility.

2.  Comparable-Domain Restriction

To ensure cross-window comparability, a common lag cap is defined:

COMMON_P_MAX = min_{window, ecdet} p_max_feasible.

Empirically:

-   Q2: COMMON_P_MAX = 3.
-   Q3: COMMON_P_MAX = 2.

The binding constraints originate in the short windows under ecdet =
“const”. The full sample does not bind comparability.

However, the crucial empirical finding is:

computed_comparable = computed for every run_tag × window × ecdet.

That is, the comparable-domain filter does not eliminate any cell that
was successfully computed by the engine. It defines a common admissible
region across windows, but it does not prune the empirically feasible
lattice.

Thus, comparable-domain discipline binds only in definition, not in
exclusion.

3.  Domain Width and Feasibility Compression

Differences in admissible domain width across regimes arise from
feasibility constraints (gate_fail) and structural impossibilities
(missing), not from comparability filtering.

Quantitatively:

-   In the full sample, the majority of lattice cells are computed
    (approximately 80–83%).
-   In Fordism under Q3, only approximately 24% of cells are computed.
-   Post-Fordism lies between these extremes.

Runtime failures are zero across all runs. The compression is therefore
not numerical instability but intrinsic finite-sample feasibility.

Stage 2 therefore distinguishes between two geometric objects:

1.  Confinement dimension (Stage 1 result): invariant rank r = 1.
2.  Admissible domain width (Stage 2 result): window-specific extent of
    feasible (p,r) combinations.

Regime heterogeneity appears in the second object, not the first.

4.  Structural Interpretation within the Confinement Framework

Within the confinement architecture, reduced rank represents dimensional
restriction of stochastic drift. Stage 1 established that this
dimensional restriction is invariant across regimes. Stage 2 now shows
that regime differentiation operates through the breadth of admissible
dynamic specifications, not through shifts in confinement dimension.

The empirical structure is therefore:

-   The long-run manifold remains one-dimensional.
-   The flexibility of admissible short-run dynamics varies by regime.
-   Higher polynomial dimension tightens admissibility in short windows
    but leaves confinement unchanged.
-   Cross-window comparability does not artificially enforce the
    selected model.

This implies that regime transformation manifests as variation in
stability margins and dynamic admissibility, rather than as a
redefinition of the structural manifold itself.

Stage 2 Conclusion

Stage 2 completes the geometric audit of the lag–rank lattice:

-   Feasibility constraints are window-specific and dimension-dependent.
-   Comparable-domain restriction is definitional and non-pruning.
-   Domain width varies across regimes.
-   Confinement dimension does not.

The admissible geometry is therefore fully characterized.

Stage 2 closed.
