Stage 1 Conclusions: Confinement Geometry and Identification Narrowing

Stage 1 establishes the geometric structure of the system prior to any
substantive interpretation. The objective was not to maximize fit nor to
privilege any particular information criterion, but to determine whether
the joint selection problem over lag order and rank admits a stable and
interpretable confinement structure.

1.  Invariance of Confinement Dimension

Across all admissible specifications—polynomial order (Q2, Q3), basis
representation (raw and orthogonal), deterministic components (const and
none), and regime windows (Fordism, Post-Fordism, Full sample)— the
unrestricted computed lattice selects (p,r) = (1,1) under both PIC and
BIC criteria. Ambiguity sets under ΔPIC ≤ 2 are singletons in every
case.

This result is not imposed by comparable-domain restrictions. The
feasible region for lag order remains strictly larger than p=1 in all
windows; the selection of p=1 therefore arises from the internal
geometry of the criterion surface rather than boundary truncation.
Similarly, the selection of r=1 is not the consequence of a degenerate
lattice: higher-rank candidates are available but never selected as
global minima.

The implication is precise: the system admits a one-dimensional
cointegrating manifold across all windows. Regime partitioning does not
alter the dimensionality of long-run confinement. Structural drift in
the four-dimensional state vector is consistently restricted by a single
stationary linear combination. Confinement dimension is therefore
invariant across regimes.

2.  Feasibility Compression and Domain Width

Although the confinement dimension is invariant, the admissible lattice
differs markedly across windows. The Fordist and Post-Fordist subsamples
exhibit substantial feasibility compression relative to the full sample.
Gate failures sharply restrict the computed cells in short windows,
especially under deterministic constants and higher polynomial dimension
(Q3).

However, comparable-domain filtering does not trim any computed cells.
For every run and window, the number of computed models equals the
number of computed comparable models. Regime differences in admissible
domain width therefore arise exclusively from feasibility constraints
rather than artificial comparability restrictions.

Regime transformation does not operate through shifts in confinement
dimension. Instead, it appears through variation in admissible dynamic
domain width—the size of the feasible (p,r) region consistent with the
data.

3.  Sharp Identification Within the Computed Lattice

The information landscape is not flat. The global PIC minimum is sharply
identified at (p,r) = (1,1) in every window and representation. BIC
yields minor near-ties in small subsamples but does not alter the global
minimum. Ambiguity sets remain singleton under standard tolerance
thresholds.

The grid search has therefore narrowed the admissible specification
space to a single confinement geometry, invariant across basis rotation
and regime partitioning.

4.  Theoretical Implication

Because rank remains invariant across Fordist and Post-Fordist windows,
regime transformation cannot be characterized as a change in the
dimensionality of the structural manifold. Instead, regime differences
must operate through shifts in adjustment orientation (alpha), changes
in short-run propagation dynamics, or alterations in stability margins
and admissible dynamic domains.

Stage 1 establishes: 1) A robust one-dimensional cointegrating manifold
(r=1) across all regimes and representations. 2) Regime differences
manifest through feasibility compression and admissible domain width,
not through rank shifts. 3) The joint selection (p,r)=(1,1) is sharply
identified and invariant to basis rotation.

These results justify proceeding to Stage 3 with a confinement-first
framework.
