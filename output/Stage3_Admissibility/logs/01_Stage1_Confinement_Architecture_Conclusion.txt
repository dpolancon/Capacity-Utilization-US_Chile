Stage 1 — Confinement Architecture from the Full Sample to Regime
Partition

Stage 1 begins with the full historical sample. The unrestricted lattice
evaluation of lag length (p) and rank (r) identifies a sharply defined
global minimum at (p=1, r=1) across all polynomial degrees (Q2, Q3),
bases (raw and orthogonalized), and deterministic specifications (const,
none). The PIC landscape heatmaps show a pronounced minimum at this
coordinate, with criterion values worsening as either p or r move away
from unity within the feasible region. No competing minima appear. The
ΔPIC ≤ 2 ambiguity sets collapse to a single cell in every
configuration.

The full historical system therefore exhibits one-dimensional long-run
confinement: the drift of the state vector is restricted by a single
stable long-run relation, and this restriction is sharply identified.
Orthogonalization leaves both the feasible lattice and the location of
the minimum unchanged, confirming that the result is invariant to
invertible polynomial reparameterizations. BIC diagnostics reproduce the
same selection, with only minor near-ties at higher ranks that do not
overturn the global optimum.

This full-sample result defines the structural baseline: the data
support a single long-run confinement relation, and the preferred
dynamic order is parsimonious (p=1).

Regime partitions are then introduced not to re-identify rank, but to
interrogate whether confinement changes when the historical sample is
split.

When the sample is partitioned into Fordism and Post-Fordism, the
location of the minimum remains unchanged: (p=1, r=1) under both PIC and
BIC for all deterministics and bases. The confinement dimension does not
shift across regimes. There is no evidence of a rank change.

What does change is the width of the admissible domain: the portion of
the (p,r) lattice that remains feasible (computed) rather than failing
admissibility gates.

The Fordist subsample exhibits severe feasibility compression.
Particularly under higher-degree specifications (Q3), large portions of
the lattice are infeasible (gate_fail or missing), and the feasible lag
region narrows. The Fordist PIC landscape appears as a thin feasible
strip, indicating limited tolerance for alternative short-run dynamics.
The long-run restriction remains one-dimensional, but the space of
dynamically admissible specifications tightens.

The Post-Fordist subsample exhibits a wider feasible region than
Fordism, though still narrower than the full sample. Feasibility expands
relative to the Fordist window without altering the rank structure. The
long-run confinement relation persists, while dynamic admissibility
loosens.

This chronological comparison supports a regulationist hypothesis. If
regimes corresponded to distinct long-run structures, one would expect
confinement dimension to shift across regime windows. Instead, rank
remains invariant, and regime differentiation appears through stability
margins and dynamic admissibility: institutional mediation tightens or
loosens which dynamic specifications can be sustained, without
redefining the dimensionality of long-run confinement.

Stage 1 therefore closes with a stable confinement architecture: a
single long-run restriction persists across regimes, while regime
differences operate through changes in feasible domain width rather than
through changes in cointegration rank.
