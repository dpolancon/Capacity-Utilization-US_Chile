# §2.2.4 — Multi-Scalar Contradictions Rule Out a Stable Desired Capacity Utilization
**Source authority:** Vidal (2014, 2019); Okishio (1961); Boyer (1990); Aglietta (1979)
**Date drafted:** 2026-04-02
**Word count:** ~510
**Self-check:** PASS
**Transition check:** Exit ramp hands off to §2.2.5 (synthesis of the four-level crisis-trigger ladder).

---

Okishio's cumulative disequilibrium operates at the aggregate level: decentralized investment decisions produce an unstable utilization path that no individual capitalist intends. But the argument asserts macro instability without explaining why the firm-level decisions that generate it cannot be internally corrected. The standard Kaleckian answer assumes they can — firms hold a stable desired capacity utilization rate that anchors investment plans, and deviations from it are self-correcting through adjustment mechanisms. Vidal's (2014, 2019) contribution dismantles this assumption from below: multi-scalar contradictions within the firm itself rule out a coherent desired utilization target, not as an empirical accident but as a structural consequence of how capitalist production is organized.

The contradiction operates simultaneously across four levels. At the mode of production, competition compels each firm to minimize unit costs through mechanization and labor intensification — decisions that are individually rational but collectively degrade the demand conditions on which all firms depend. At the meso and spatial scale, sectoral and geographic disproportionalities mean that individual investment decisions face an environment structured by imbalances they cannot observe, let alone correct. At the aggregate level, coordination failures in the timing of capacity additions ensure that the demand signal each firm responds to has already shifted by the time new capacity comes online. At the firm level — and this is Vidal's distinctive contribution — the contradiction is irreducible: the imperative to discipline labor and extract maximum output in the short run conflicts with the imperative to develop workers' cognitive capacities for coordination, flexibility, and learning over longer horizons. Shareholders, creditors, supply-chain counterparts, and operational managers pull the utilization decision in incompatible directions within the same organizational unit. There is no planning horizon at which these imperatives can be simultaneously satisfied, and therefore no coherent desired utilization rate from which to construct an investment anchor.

The market is anarchic because social production is not consciously coordinated. What Vidal establishes is that the firm does not overcome this anarchy — it internalizes it through constrained planning shaped by uncertainty and contradictory interests across organizational scales. This gives Okishio's crisis trigger a two-level foundation: no stable within-firm target from which to aggregate, and no coordination mechanism between firms. The cumulative disequilibrium of the utilization path is grounded at both levels simultaneously.

The implication for the chapter's empirical strategy is direct. If firms held a stable desired utilization rate, the capacity utilization coefficient in the FM investment specification — what Stage C estimates as the behavioral response of accumulation to demand-side conditions — should be well-defined and stable across sub-periods. Vidal's argument rules this out: the coefficient is not a structural parameter but a regime-specific behavioral response, contingent on the institutional settlement within which firms operate. The Fordist accumulation regime temporarily stabilized the within-firm contradiction. High effective demand, a regulated wage relation, and monopoly pricing conventions — what the regulation school (Boyer 1990; Aglietta 1979) interprets as an institutional resolution of the capital-labor antagonism — aligned the competing imperatives sufficiently to produce a *de facto* normal utilization rate. Not because the firm became internally unified, but because the external institutional settlement suppressed multi-scalar tensions long enough for planning horizons to extend. The demise of that settlement re-releases the contradiction, and CUSUM instability of the utilization coefficient across sub-periods would confirm this reading empirically. What the next subsection assembles is the four-level ladder that connects these mechanisms into a single theoretical architecture — the structure within which the chapter's three empirical stages operate.
