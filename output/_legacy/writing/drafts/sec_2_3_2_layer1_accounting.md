## §2.3.2 Layer 1: The Accounting Foundation

Everything in this subsection is a pure accounting identity. No behavioral equation is imposed; no parameter requires estimation. The decomposition structures what *can* be tested in Stage C --- it does not pre-empt the result.

The profit rate on corporate capital decomposes exhaustively into three factors:

$$
r \;\equiv\; \frac{\Pi}{K} \;=\; \underbrace{\frac{Y}{Y^p}}_{\mu} \;\cdot\; \underbrace{\frac{Y^p}{K}}_{b} \;\cdot\; \underbrace{\frac{\Pi}{Y}}_{\pi} \;=\; \mu \cdot b \cdot \pi
\tag{2.x}
$$

| Factor | Symbol | Definition | Channel |
|---|---|---|---|
| Capacity utilization | $\mu = Y/Y^p$ | Demand realization | Demand |
| Capital productivity | $b = Y^p/K$ | Technology at normal capacity | Technology |
| Profit share | $\pi = \Pi/Y$ | Distributive conflict | Distribution |

Each factor indexes a structurally distinct determinant of profitability. Capacity utilization $\mu$ registers the gap between realized output and the productive capacity established in §2.3.1; capital productivity $b$ captures the technological efficiency of the capital stock evaluated at normal utilization; the profit share $\pi$ registers the distributional outcome of the wage--profit conflict. No factor is reducible to another, and no factor is residual --- the decomposition is exhaustive by construction.

The demand-led content of the identity is immediate. Define normal-capacity profitability as $r^n \equiv b\pi$, the profit rate that would obtain if the capital stock operated at its design capacity. Then $r = \mu \times r^n$. Capitalists accumulate on the basis of $r$, not $r^n$ --- realized profitability carries a demand signal that normal-capacity profitability, by definition, filters out. The distinction is not merely analytical: it determines whether a falling profit rate registers a distributional squeeze, a technological slowdown, or a demand shortfall, and each diagnosis implies a different crisis mechanism.

The gross accumulation identity nests the Weisskopf decomposition within the investment decision:

$$
g_K \;\equiv\; \frac{I}{K} \;=\; \underbrace{\frac{I}{\Pi}}_{\chi} \;\cdot\; \underbrace{\frac{\Pi}{Y}}_{\pi} \;\cdot\; \underbrace{\frac{Y}{Y^p}}_{\mu} \;\cdot\; \underbrace{\frac{Y^p}{K}}_{b} \;=\; \chi \cdot \pi \cdot \mu \cdot b
\tag{2.y}
$$

where $\chi \equiv I/\Pi$ is the recapitalization rate --- the share of profits ploughed back into fixed capital. The Cambridge equation closes the system:

$$
\kappa \;=\; \chi\, r \;-\; \delta
\tag{2.z}
$$

Three distinct objects appear: the market profit rate $r$, which is an outcome; the recapitalization rate $\chi$, which is a decision; and the depreciation rate $\delta$, which is a physical parameter. Stage C admits two dependent variables that correspond to different theoretical objects --- the capital accumulation rate $\kappa_t = I/K$ (Robinson's convention, registering the pace of capacity expansion) and the recapitalization rate $a_t = \chi_t = I/\Pi$ (Foley 1982, registering the capitalist's reinvestment decision). Both are estimable; each foregrounds a different side of the accumulation circuit.

The implication for investment-function estimation is therefore precise. The accounting identity identifies three structurally independent channels --- demand ($\mu$), technology ($b$), and distribution ($\pi$) --- through which profitability transmits to accumulation. Compressing them *ex ante* into the scalar $r$, as the Keynes--Robinson specification does, suppresses exactly the regime-relevant information that distinguishes a demand-driven crisis from a distributional squeeze. Layer 1 therefore requires that the three channels enter the investment function separately by default. Whether compression is warranted is an empirical question --- one that the Wald tests in §2.7.6 are designed to adjudicate.
