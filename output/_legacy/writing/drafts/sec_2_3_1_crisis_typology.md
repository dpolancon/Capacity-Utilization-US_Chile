# §2.3.1 — Stagnation and Crisis: A Typology
**Draft — 2026-04-02 | ~290 words**

---

Not every downward pressure on profitability constitutes a crisis, and not every crisis is structural. The distinction matters because the empirical stages that follow must identify *which* stagnation tendencies operate in each sub-period and whether they compound into a crisis that the accumulation regime $\Lambda$ cannot absorb. Vidal (2014, 2019) provides the operative categories.

A **stagnation tendency** is a sustained downward pressure on the rate of profit — a force internal to the accumulation process that depresses $r$ through one or more of its channels ($\mu$, $b$, $\pi$). Stagnation tendencies can coexist and partially offset one another: rising profit share may compensate falling capital productivity for a time, deferring the aggregate outcome. A **partial crisis** interrupts the economy-wide circuit of capital but remains resolvable through institutional reconfiguration within the existing accumulation regime — a devaluation, a shift in trade policy, a wage settlement. A **structural crisis** is a deep and prolonged interruption that partial adjustments cannot resolve; it requires drastic restructuring of $\Lambda$ itself.

The content of each category depends on which channel transmits the pressure. Basu (2019) classifies crisis theories along two axes — the side of the capital circuit (demand versus financial) and the direction of surplus value (excess versus deficit). Recast in Vidal's terminology, the four cells define distinct stagnation tendencies:

|  | *Excess of Surplus Value* | *Deficit of Surplus Value* |
|---|---|---|
| **Demand side** | Under-consumption / realization crisis | Profit squeeze |
| **Financial side** | Financial fragility | Falling normal rate of profit |

Each cell corresponds to a specific configuration of the Weisskopf channels: under-consumption operates through $\mu$, profit squeeze through $\pi$, and falling normal profitability through $b$ (or $b\pi$ jointly). Financial fragility operates through the recapitalization rate $\chi$ — the channel that converts profitability into accumulation.

This grid is not a menu of competing theories. It is the interpretive template through which Stage B identifies the dominant stagnation tendency per sub-period and Stage C determines which channel drives the recapitalization decision. The accounting foundation that gives each cell its formal content is the subject of §2.3.2.
