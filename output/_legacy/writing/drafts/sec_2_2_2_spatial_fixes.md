# §2.2.2 — Disproportionality, Class Struggle, and Spatial Fixes

The stagnation typology identifies four crisis tendencies but leaves open the question of how capitalism defers them. Giovanni Arrighi and David Harvey provide the answer: the contradictions of over-accumulation are resolved — temporarily and incompletely — through spatial fixes that displace surplus capital across geography and forward across time. The Fordist era (1945–1978) is the historical settlement within which those fixes operated, and its dissolution is the moment at which they ceased to operate for both the center and the periphery this chapter studies.

Arrighi (1978) locates the crisis mechanism in disproportionality between the production and realization of surplus value, generating two distinct pressures on the profit rate. Downward wage pressure produces demand deficiency and realization failure; upward wage pressure produces profit squeeze and an accumulation bottleneck. In both cases, concentration and centralization of capital accelerate during crisis — strengthening the collective power of capital but simultaneously consolidating the organizational leverage of labor. The state absorbs excess capital through unproductive expenditure — military spending, social reproduction, infrastructure — without resolving the distributional contradiction that generated the surplus in the first place.

Harvey (2003, 2018) generalizes this into a theory of spatio-temporal displacement. Capital resolves over-accumulation through three mechanisms: temporal deferral via the credit system, which bridges gaps in the circuit of capital; spatial reallocation through investment in built environments and geographical restructuring; and the construction of institutional settlements that stabilize accumulation temporarily by fixing the terms of class compromise. The Fordist settlement combined all three: US hegemony underwrote the dollar standard, Bretton Woods regulated capital flows, and import-substitution industrialization in the periphery absorbed surplus through protected domestic markets. The center accumulated through financial expansion; the periphery accumulated under balance-of-payments constraint and foreign-exchange scarcity. The ISI model was itself a contested spatial fix — it deferred the external constraint without eliminating it, incubating the conditions for a more violent reversal when the fix closed.

The fix closed asymmetrically. For Chile, the balance-of-payments constraint tightened as copper revenues fluctuated and import dependence deepened, surfacing the distributional contradictions that ISI had contained. For the United States, the Vietnam War fiscal expansion and the collapse of the Bretton Woods settlement exposed the internal contradiction between wage militancy and profit-rate maintenance that the Fordist compact had managed. In both cases, the closure of the spatial fix forced the distributive conflict back into the domestic accumulation path — and it is the *path* of capacity utilization through which that conflict registers empirically.
