Every existing estimate of capacity utilization in the heterodox profitability literature imposes a unit transformation elasticity by construction. The HP filter forces $\theta = 1$ because its trend-cycle decomposition mechanically attributes all output variation to demand fluctuations around a smooth technological path; peak-to-peak interpolation does the same by defining potential output as a deterministic envelope of observed maxima. Chapter 1 demonstrated the empirical consequences: the Federal Reserve Board series — the instrument that both Sraffian-convergence and classical-Marxian traditions rely on — was built to produce a stable signal for monetary policy governance, not to recover a structural relationship between demand and technology. Its stationarity is a design feature. When that series enters the Weisskopf decomposition $r = \mu b \pi$, the conflation of $\mu_t$ and $b_t$ is not an approximation but a category error: the demand channel and the technology channel collapse into a single residual, and the decomposition loses the capacity to distinguish accumulation dynamics from distributional shifts.

This chapter asks what capacity utilization does once it is correctly identified. The question is not whether $\mu_t$ matters for profitability — the accounting identity guarantees that it does — but whether the *path* of $\mu_t$ operates as an independent crisis trigger in the recapitalization decision, and whether that mechanism differs structurally between center and periphery. The theoretical basis is Okishio (1961): decentralized capitalist decision-making produces cumulative disequilibrium in aggregate demand because capacity utilization is intrinsically unstable, always reversing toward normal capacity but overshooting. Periods of overaccumulation drive $\mu_t$ above normal, inventories accumulate, and the eventual downward correction contracts accumulation demand with a violence proportional to the preceding excess. Spatio-temporal fixes (Harvey 2003) can defer this reversal across space and time — but they incubate the conditions for a more severe structural crisis when the institutional settlement that sustained them breaks down.

The chapter makes three contributions. First, it provides a first-stage structural identification of $\hat{\mu}_t$ from a four-variable cointegrated system in $(y, k, \pi, \pi k)$, where the transformation elasticity $\hat{\theta}_t = \theta_1 + \theta_2 \pi_t$ is distribution-conditioned and estimated rather than imposed. The confinement vector $y - \theta_1 k - \theta_2(\pi k) = \hat{\mu}$ recovers capacity utilization as a cointegrating residual — a long-run disequilibrium measure, not a stationary cycle. Second, it extends the identification to the capitalist periphery through Stage A.2: a cost-minimization problem on the mechanization possibility frontier under balance-of-payments constraint, where the capital composition split ($K^{NR}$ and $K^{ME}$) and the mechanization gap between center and periphery are derived from the structural parameters rather than imposed by assumption. Third, it estimates a behavioral investment function grounded in the full Weisskopf disaggregation — the FM specification enters the three channels $(\mu, b, \pi)$ separately as the Layer-1 default, and the Okishio crisis-trigger hypothesis is tested as a Wald restriction $(\psi_1 = \psi_3)$ within that framework: whether capacity utilization enters the recapitalization decision with the same coefficient as capital productivity, as the compressed profit rate would require, or operates as an independent channel with distinct behavioral content.

The primary estimation period is 1945–1978, the Fordist era in the United States and the Inward-Looking Accumulation Regime in Chile. Both datasets are the author's own constructions: the US panel is built from BEA national income and fixed asset tables via the `US-BEA-Income-FixedAssets-Dataset` repository; the Chilean capital stock is a stock-flow consistent harmonization of the Pérez (1900–1994) canonical baseline and the Central Bank extension, maintained in the `K-Stock-Harmonization` repository.

The chapter proceeds as follows. Section 2.2 reviews the Marxist crisis theory literature that grounds the crisis-trigger concept and locates the post-Keynesian utilization controversy within it. Section 2.3 establishes the two-layer analytical framework — the accounting foundation and its behavioral interpretation — that organizes all three empirical stages. Section 2.4 documents the data. Section 2.5 presents Stage A: the MPF cost-minimization and the four-variable VECM identification of $\hat{\mu}_t$, $\hat{b}_t$, and $\hat{\theta}_t$ for both countries. Section 2.6 applies the Weisskopf profitability decomposition using Stage A outputs. Section 2.7 estimates the investment function. Section 2.8 reports results. Section 2.9 concludes.
