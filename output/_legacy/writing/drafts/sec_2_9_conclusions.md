This chapter set out to answer a structural question: does the path of capacity utilization operate as an independent crisis trigger in the demise of the Fordist accumulation regime, or does it reduce to the profit rate it enters? The answer required three things that did not previously exist in a single framework — a structurally identified $\mu_t$ that is not stationarized by construction, a profitability decomposition that separates the demand channel from the technology channel cleanly enough to attribute stagnation tendencies, and a behavioral investment function whose coefficients can be tested against the accounting benchmark that generates them. The chapter delivers all three.

Four contributions therefore stand independently of the specific point estimates:

1. **Structural CU identification without stationarity imposition.** The Cajas-Guijarro FOC within the four-variable VECM recovers $\hat{\mu}_t$ and $\hat{b}_t$ as separate objects — center and periphery compared within the same framework. No prior work identifies CU from a cost-minimization problem embedded in a cointegrated system that permits $\mu_t$ to be I(1).

2. **Peripheral extension with capital composition and BoP constraint.** Stage A.2 derives the center-periphery mechanization gap from the FOC of a BoP-constrained cost-minimization problem over $K^{NR} + K^{ME}$, making the Kaldor-ECLA fault line — BoP ceiling vs. internal consumption drain — an estimable structural question rather than an interpretive assertion.

3. **Accounting-grounded investment function with Okishio Wald test.** The FM spec enters the three Weisskopf channels separately because the accumulation identity requires it, and the Okishio test is identified precisely because Stage A delivers the clean channel separation that HP filtering destroys. This is the first contribution to test Okishio's (1961) crisis-trigger notion within an ARDL framework using structurally identified CU regressors.

4. **Firm-level micro-foundation for aggregate CU instability.** Multi-scalar contradictions within the firm rule out a stable desired utilization rate prior to and independently of between-firm competition, giving Okishio's macro-level cumulative disequilibrium a two-level foundation. CUSUM instability of $\psi_3$ across sub-periods is the empirical signature of this claim.

Chapter 3 inherits $\hat{\mu}_t$, $\hat{b}_t$, and $\hat{\theta}_t$ as given inputs — no re-estimation. The Weisskopf decomposition and the Okishio crisis-trigger results become the structural objects that Ch3's local projections and counterfactual exercises interrogate. Demand conditions intervene on the political economy of the ceiling, i.e. the class struggle over distribution, utilization, and the institutional forms that govern whether accumulation can proceed.
