# Stage S1 Write-up (System Identification in the lnY–lnK VECM)

Stage S1 marks the transition from conditional replication to system identification. The empirical exercise is no longer framed as “estimating potential output” or extracting an output gap, but as testing whether accumulation and effective output exhibit restricted drift: whether trajectories in \((\ln Y_t,\ln K_t)\) remain confined to a stable long-run manifold under historically specific institutional mediation.

The first result of S1 is not a coefficient but a structural regularity in short-run architecture. Across deterministic treatments, the preferred specification allocates short-run memory to accumulation rather than to output’s own inertia, and it does so at minimal lag depth. In other words, the system repeatedly selects a sparse short-run map in which \(\Delta\ln K_t\) carries the short-run dynamics while \(\Delta\ln Y_t\) does not require a rich lag structure. This outcome is best read as a property of memory allocation under confinement: the system chooses where to spend degrees of freedom to preserve bounded drift.

The second result is a correction to any single-equation interpretation of adjustment. In conditional error-correction models, adjustment is measured through a single coefficient in the output equation. The system representation shows that this is incomplete: deviation from the long-run restriction is absorbed through joint adjustment, with both output and accumulation participating in the correction mechanism. This does not deny the centrality of accumulation in propagation; it specifies that the long-run correction pressure cannot be represented as “output adjusts while capital is given.” The system distributes the burden of restoring confinement across both coordinates of the state vector.

S1 becomes saturated only once the analysis moves beyond winners. The system is evaluated over a lattice of admissible specifications: short-run memory depth can increase, and its allocation can vary across structured profiles; deterministic anchoring can also vary by family. The lattice reveals that the estimator can generate multiple candidate long-run “tilts” once additional parameters are allowed to reassign persistence between the short-run map and deterministic drift handling. At higher lag depth and under more permissive deterministic environments, the long-run mapping can rotate sharply, producing both high-tilt and low-tilt candidate manifolds among well-ranked specifications. These rotations are not dismissed as numerical noise. They are treated as specification risk: evidence of the manifold angles that become feasible when drift and persistence are redistributed across parameter blocks.

Two gates discipline this risk map. First, admissibility constraints filter out candidates that violate the expected stochastic structure even when their stability indicators appear attractive. Second, the likelihood–complexity frontier imposes a cost on parameterization: some rotations are feasible and admissible but are purchased by complexity rather than revealed by stable co-movement. The resulting identification logic is comparative–relational rather than confirmatory: the task is to map feasible rotations and then justify why the dominant confinement corner is selected—because it remains stable under local perturbations and is not an artifact of overparameterization or drift laundering.

Within this framework, deterministic families define clusters of interpretation. A fit-dominant family exhibits a stable manifold neighborhood: the long-run tilt remains essentially unchanged under small perturbations of short-run structure, indicating a robust confinement mapping. A stability-buffered family achieves a larger stability margin through deterministic structure, making it useful as a robustness check that demonstrates how stability can be “bought” without changing the dominant short-run allocation pattern. Trend-heavy or otherwise permissive deterministic families function as stress tests: they expand the space of feasible rotations and thereby clarify the extent to which drift can be absorbed into deterministic components at the cost of altering the inferred manifold. They are not treated as baselines unless they deliver interior, locally stable identification.

The error-correction term is interpreted accordingly. In a rank-1 confinement setting, the ECT is the distance-from-manifold coordinate: a pressure index that measures deviations from the long-run mapping. Its shape over time can be evaluated for robustness within a stable specification neighborhood. Its level, however, is not inherently comparable across specifications because deterministic anchoring and normalization conventions shift the coordinate system. As a result, utilization is not taken to be directly identified as a level series by the raw residual. The ECT is reported and interpreted primarily as a normalized pressure coordinate, with level interpretation requiring an explicit anchoring convention for “normal” capacity.

Stage S1 therefore delivers a saturated system-level object: a dominant confinement corner characterized by sparse, accumulation-loaded short-run propagation; joint adjustment that distributes correction pressure across output and accumulation; a mapped space of feasible manifold rotations disciplined by admissibility and by the likelihood–complexity frontier; and an interpretation rule that treats the ECT as a pressure coordinate rather than a mechanically identified utilization level. This closes the bivariate confinement stage as an identification exercise: not by selecting a single number, but by establishing which aspects of the geometry are invariant and which vary with specification structure.
