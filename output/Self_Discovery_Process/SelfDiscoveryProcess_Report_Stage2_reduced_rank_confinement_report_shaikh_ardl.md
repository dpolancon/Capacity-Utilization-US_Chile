## 1. Object of Analysis: Confinement Rather Than Potential Output

The empirical object is not a production function and not a mechanical output-gap decomposition. The starting identity is:

ln Y\_t = ln Y^p\_t + ln u\_t

Capacity is not a trend component imposed ex ante. Instead, the question is whether the data exhibit bounded co-movement between accumulation (K) and effective output (Y) once institutional level shifts are accounted for. In reduced-rank terms, the issue is whether stochastic drift is confined to a lower-dimensional manifold.

## 2. Implicit Reduced-Rank Structure in the ARDL(2,4) Anchor

State vector:

X\_t = (ln Y\_t, ln K\_t)'

Cointegration implies rank r = 1. The confinement manifold is defined by:

β'X\_t = ln Y\_t − a − θ ln K\_t − Σ c\_h D\_h

Estimated long-run slope:

θ = 0.7085689 (SE = 0.04319, t = 16.41)

This θ is the tilt of the confinement hyperplane. It partitions output into:

• A capacity component mapped from accumulation
• A utilization component absorbing deviations from that mapping

The empirical question becomes whether drift remains restricted to this hyperplane.

## 3. Technological–Distributive Interpretation of θ < 1

θ < 1 implies sub-proportional mapping from accumulation to capacity.

Within the confinement framework, θ summarizes a historically specific technological–distributive configuration (Λ). Accumulation does not mechanically generate commensurate productive capacity. Effective demand can outrun technological transformation, making utilization the slack variable.

Thus θ is not a production elasticity. It is a regime-specific transformation parameter.

λ = −0.159747 (t = −4.125)

λ measures the rate at which short-run dynamics correct deviations from the confinement manifold. This is not equilibrium restoration. It is institutional mediation ensuring bounded drift.

The strength of λ provides operational evidence that the rank-1 restriction is active in the data.

## Deepened Theoretical Write-Up

### Confinement, Demand Formation, and Technological–Distributive Mediation

The empirical replication does more than recover a long-run multiplier. It identifies a structural confinement relation between accumulation and effective output. In a two-variable integrated system, reduced rank (r = 1) implies that stochastic trends are not independent. Output and capital do not drift freely; they co-move along a one-dimensional manifold.

This manifold is not a production frontier. It is the historically mediated transformation rule through which accumulation is converted into productive capacity under a specific institutional configuration. The slope parameter θ measures the tilt of this confinement surface. Its estimate below unity implies that the technological–distributive regime does not translate capital accumulation proportionally into capacity expansion.

Within the confinement framework, this has a precise meaning: demand formation and distributive structure jointly condition how accumulation becomes productive capacity. The parameter θ summarizes this mediation. It is neither purely technological nor purely distributive; it reflects their interaction.

The adjustment coefficient λ indicates how deviations from this manifold are absorbed. A significant and negative λ confirms that short-run dynamics correct divergence from the technological–distributive constraint. Approximately sixteen percent of disequilibrium is absorbed annually. The system therefore exhibits bounded drift, not equilibrium convergence.

This interpretation shifts the ontological status of utilization. Utilization is not an exogenous gap; it is the residual deviation from the confinement manifold. When deterministic regime shifts are embedded in the long-run relation, they translate the manifold rather than generating volatility in utilization. This confirms that structural breaks operate as shifts in the technological–distributive configuration rather than as pure demand shocks.

From the standpoint of your broader theoretical architecture:

1. Confinement dimension remains invariant (r = 1).
2. The manifold tilt (θ) encodes regime-specific mediation.
3. Adjustment speed (λ) reflects institutional stabilization capacity.
4. Deterministic shifts represent historical re-anchoring of the regime.

The ARDL representation is therefore a conditional expression of a reduced-rank system. The next logical step is to verify confinement through a full Johansen system, recover the α vector, and determine whether adjustment is distributed asymmetrically across output and accumulation.

If the system confirms rank-1 confinement and similar θ, then the empirical evidence supports the thesis that capitalist growth in this window is governed by a stable technological–distributive constraint. If rank instability or manifold rotation emerges, that would indicate regime transformation rather than stable confinement.

This reframing aligns the empirical object directly with your confinement theory: demand does not simply fluctuate around capacity; it operates within a technologically and distributively mediated constraint that shapes the mapping from accumulation to output.
