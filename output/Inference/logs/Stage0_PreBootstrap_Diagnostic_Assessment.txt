4.X Pre-Bootstrap Diagnostic Assessment

Before conducting bootstrap-based rank inference, a diagnostic
assessment of the maintained VECM specification is performed. The
objective is limited and procedural: to verify that the short-run
dynamics are adequately specified for inference and to document the
disturbance environment that will shape the finite-sample distribution
of the likelihood ratio statistic. Stage 0 does not deliver conclusions
regarding rank, parameters, or regime interpretation. It evaluates
whether the maintained specification is bootstrappable.

Full Sample

Across all specifications (Q2 and Q3 polynomial blocks, raw and
orthogonal bases, and both deterministic cases), residual serial
correlation is not detected at conventional portmanteau horizons (K = 4,
8). The baseline lag order p = 1 satisfies the serial-correlation gate
uniformly. No repair of the lag structure is required. The maintained
short-run skeleton is therefore admissible for inference.

The disturbance structure, however, is markedly non-Gaussian. Average
residual kurtosis is approximately 5.7, substantially above the Gaussian
benchmark of 3. Conditional heteroskedasticity tests indicate pervasive
variance instability across components (ARCH flags present in
essentially all specifications). These features do not invalidate
estimation but imply that asymptotic Gaussian critical values are
unreliable in finite samples. The disturbance environment of the full
sample is characterized by variance clustering and heavy tails. This
motivates the use of wild bootstrap procedures in Stage 1.

Fordist Subsample

In the Fordist window, the serial-correlation gate is again satisfied at
p = 1 across all specifications. No dynamic misspecification is
detected.

The distributional environment differs from the full sample. Average
kurtosis is approximately 3.2, close to the Gaussian benchmark. Variance
clustering remains present (ARCH detection persists in most components),
but tail thickness is substantially reduced relative to the full sample.
The Fordist disturbance environment thus appears as one of bounded
variance instability rather than extreme tail behavior. Variance is
non-constant, yet shocks are less explosive in magnitude.

Post-Fordist Subsample

The post-Fordist window likewise satisfies the serial-correlation gate
at p = 1. The short-run dynamics remain admissible.

In contrast to Fordism, residual kurtosis increases to approximately
5.1, indicating heavier tails and more extreme realizations. Conditional
heteroskedasticity remains detectable, although the share of components
flagged by ARCH-type tests is somewhat lower than in Fordism. This
pattern suggests that variance instability in the post-Fordist period
may manifest less as smooth clustering and more as episodic turbulence.
The disturbance environment is therefore characterized by heightened
tail risk relative to the Fordist regime.

Diagnostic Synthesis

Three conclusions follow from Stage 0.

First, no evidence of residual serial correlation is detected in any
window. The maintained VECM structure with p = 1 is dynamically
admissible and suitable for bootstrap recursion.

Second, conditional heteroskedasticity is pervasive in all windows.
Gaussian asymptotic critical values are therefore inappropriate for
finite-sample rank testing.

Third, disturbance characteristics differ across regimes: the Fordist
period exhibits variance clustering with relatively moderate tails,
whereas the post-Fordist period exhibits heavier tails and greater
episodic volatility. These differences affect the finite-sample
distribution of the likelihood ratio statistic but do not alter the
maintained specification of the system.

Accordingly, Stage 1 rank inference proceeds using wild bootstrap
procedures under the maintained deterministic specification. Rank
conclusions will be drawn from bootstrap-based critical values rather
than asymptotic Gaussian theory.
