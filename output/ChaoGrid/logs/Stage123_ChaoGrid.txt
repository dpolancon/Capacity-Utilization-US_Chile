
Stage 1 is about identifying the dimensionality and robustness of the system’s long-run confinement before doing any substantive historical interpretation. Concretely, it exhaustively evaluates the admissible (p, r) lattice across the full sample and regime partitions to determine whether the data support zero, one, or multiple long-run restrictions, and whether that conclusion is stable to polynomial degree, orthogonalization, and deterministic choices. The result is that the system is consistently and sharply identified as having a single long-run restriction (r = 1, p = 1), with regime differences appearing not as changes in rank but as changes in admissible dynamic width. In short, Stage 1 establishes the structural geometry of long-run confinement and verifies that it is invariant across regimes.

Stage 2 is about the geometry of admissibility of your model across historical regimes. While Stage 1 established that the system’s long-run structure is confined to a one-dimensional cointegrating manifold (rank 𝑟=1) in every sample, Stage 2 asks a different question: how wide is the dynamically feasible model space in each regime? By comparing the full sample to Fordism and Post-Fordism partitions, Stage 2 shows that the confinement dimension remains constant, but the admissible lag domain shrinks sharply in the shorter regime windows—especially under Fordism. In other words, regime change does not alter the dimensionality of the structural manifold; it alters the stability margins and dynamic flexibility of the system. The full sample exhibits a wide feasible lattice, whereas Fordism displays severe feasibility compression. Thus, Stage 2 demonstrates that institutional regimes operate through changes in admissible dynamics, not through changes in long-run rank.

Stage 3 evaluates whether the invariant long-run accumulation manifold identified in Stage 1 and geometrically mapped in Stage 2 is economically operative within each historical regime. It does so by imposing a double admissibility gate: first, combined cointegration tests to verify that a statistically coherent long-run relation exists; second, ARDL bounds tests to determine whether that relation generates an admissible error-correction mechanism. Unlike Stage 1, which identifies structural confinement, and Stage 2, which measures the width of feasible dynamics, Stage 3 asks whether the system actually adjusts toward its long-run constraint in each regime. The results show that cointegration is robust in the full sample and especially under Fordism, while Post-Fordism requires richer dynamic structures to sustain admissibility, indicating not a change in structural rank but a transformation in stability margins and propagation mechanisms. In regulationist terms, Stage 3 tests whether the mode of regulation allows the structural law of motion to be dynamically realized.
