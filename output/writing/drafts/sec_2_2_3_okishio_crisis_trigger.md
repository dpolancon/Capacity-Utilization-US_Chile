# §2.2.3 — Okishio's Crisis Trigger: Cumulative Causation through Capacity Utilization
**Source authority:** Okishio (1961); Weisskopf (1979)
**Date drafted:** 2026-04-02
**Word count:** ~460
**Self-check:** PASS
**Transition check:** Exit ramp hands off to §2.2.4 (firm-level multi-scalar contradiction that rules out stable desired CU).

---

The stagnation typology of §2.2.1 identifies the structural coordinates within which crises unfold, but it does not specify a trigger. Nobuo Okishio's 1961 contribution provides the answer that organizes this chapter: decentralized capitalist investment decisions produce a secular cumulative disequilibrium in capacity utilization, and it is the *path* of $\mu_t$ — not a static threshold — that triggers the transition from partial adjustment to structural crisis.

Individual capitalists, responding to current profitability signals, expand capacity in ways that are locally rational but globally destabilizing. When utilization is high, investment responds — but the resulting capacity additions arrive after the aggregate demand conditions that motivated them have shifted. The consequence is a secular tendency for $\mu_t$ to trace an unstable path: episodes of high utilization attract investment that eventually depresses utilization, while episodes of low utilization discourage investment and allow demand to recover, but each cycle leaves a residue of structural imbalance that the next cannot fully absorb. Utilization rates reverse toward normal capacity but overshoot, producing stagnation episodes of increasing severity. The disequilibrium is cumulative, and its accumulation is the crisis.

The channel through which the utilization path transmits into crisis runs through profitability. The Weisskopf (1979) decomposition provides the accounting identity that makes the transmission legible:

$$r_t \;\equiv\; \frac{Y_t}{Y^p_t} \cdot \frac{Y^p_t}{K_t} \cdot \frac{\Pi_t}{Y_t} \;=\; \mu_t \cdot b_t \cdot \pi_t. \tag{2.3}$$

Effective profitability is demand-led through $\mu_t$: utilization scales normal-capacity profitability $b_t \pi_t$ up or down, so the entire profit rate fluctuates with the utilization path. When Okishio's cumulative disequilibrium tilts toward chronic underutilization, profitability erodes even if the profit share $\pi_t$ remains stable and capital productivity $b_t$ does not deteriorate. The crisis is transmitted from the demand side to the profitability side through the utilization channel, and from profitability to accumulation through the investment response. Differentiating logarithmically yields the empirical Weisskopf identity that Stage B applies directly:

$$\frac{\dot{r}}{r} \;=\; \frac{\dot{\mu}}{\mu} \;+\; \frac{\dot{b}}{b} \;+\; \frac{\dot{\pi}}{\pi}. \tag{2.4}$$

Each component of the profit rate contributes additively to its growth rate: the demand channel ($\dot{\mu}/\mu$), the technology channel ($\dot{b}/b$), and the distribution channel ($\dot{\pi}/\pi$). The Weisskopf decomposition is an accounting tautology — it becomes an empirical statement only when $\mu_t$ and $b_t$ are separately identified. This is where the standard approach collapses. HP-filtering and peak-output interpolation impose $\theta = 1$ by construction, forcing the capacity manifold to track capital accumulation one-for-one and thereby collapsing the demand and technology channels into a single composite. The Okishio test — whether the *path* of $\mu_t$ operates as an independent crisis trigger or merely co-moves with $b_t$ — becomes impossible under any method that conflates the two. What Stage A resolves is precisely this identification problem: it recovers $\hat{\mu}_t$ and $\hat{b}_t$ from the long-run reproduction structure without imposing balanced growth, making (2.4) empirically operational as a three-channel decomposition.

The mechanism requires one further element. Okishio's argument asserts aggregate instability from the macro side, but it does not explain why the firm-level decisions that generate cumulative disequilibrium cannot be internally corrected. The next subsection provides the micro-foundation: multi-scalar contradictions within the firm rule out a stable desired utilization rate, closing the gap between individual investment decisions and aggregate instability.
