# §2.5.3 — First-Stage $\hat{\theta}$ Identification via Long-Run Projection

---

The cost-minimization problem of §2.5.2 yields a transformation elasticity that depends on the profit share: $\hat{\theta}_t = \theta_1 + \theta_2 \pi_t$. But which profit share? Technique choice is a long-run decision. Capitalists selecting the capital-labor ratio respond to the equilibrium distribution — the profit share consistent with the structural relationship between distribution and capacity utilization — not to transitory business-cycle fluctuations in $\pi_t$. Using the observed $\pi_t$ directly to construct $\hat{\theta}_t$ displaces short-run distributional noise into the technology channel, contaminating the identification of productive capacity $Y_t^p = B_0 K_t^{\hat{\theta}}$. What should be demand-side variation — cyclical deviations of the profit share from its long-run path — instead enters the capacity manifold as spurious time-variation in the transformation elasticity. The identification problem is to extract the component of $\pi_t$ that belongs to the technology channel and to confine the remainder to the demand channel where it structurally originates.

The first-stage identification resolves this problem by projecting $\pi_t$ onto the long-run manifold defined jointly by the cointegrating relations of the four-variable system. On the long-run manifold, the error-correction deviations vanish: CV1 and CV2 hold simultaneously as exact equalities rather than as stochastic attractors. From CV1, capacity utilization on the long-run manifold satisfies $\hat{\mu}_t^{LR} = y_t^{LR} - (\theta_1 + \theta_2 \pi_t^{LR})k_t^{LR}$; from CV2, the equilibrium Phillips curve anchors the profit share to utilization through $\pi_t^{LR} = \varrho \hat{\mu}_t^{LR}$. Substituting the first into the second and solving for the long-run profit share yields

$$\hat{\pi}_t^{LR} \;=\; \frac{\varrho\bigl(y_t^{LR} \;-\; \theta_1\, k_t^{LR}\bigr)}{1 \;+\; \varrho\, \theta_2\, k_t^{LR}}, \tag{2.14}$$

where $(y_t^{LR},\, k_t^{LR})$ are the long-run fitted components of the VECM — the portions of log output and log capital attributable to the common stochastic trends rather than to error-correction adjustment. The formula is nonlinear in the structural parameters $\theta_1$, $\theta_2$, and $\varrho$, all of which are identified from the restricted cointegrating vectors of §2.5.1. The denominator $1 + \varrho\theta_2 k_t^{LR}$ reflects the feedback loop between distribution and capacity: a higher $\theta_2$ means that technique choice is more sensitive to the profit share, which in turn means that the equilibrium profit share consistent with CV1 and CV2 simultaneously is more tightly constrained by the capital stock.

The identification proceeds in two steps:

1. **Estimate the restricted VECM** on the state vector $\mathbf{X}_t = (y_t,\, k_t,\, \pi_t,\, \pi_t k_t)'$ with $\theta_1$, $\theta_2$, $\varrho$, and $\delta$ constrained equal across CV1–CV3. Extract the long-run fitted values $(y_t^{LR},\, k_t^{LR})$ from the Gonzalo-Granger permanent component or, equivalently, from the common-trends representation of the estimated system.
2. **Construct the first-stage transformation elasticity** $\hat{\theta}_t^{(1)} = \theta_1 + \theta_2 \hat{\pi}_t^{LR}$ using equation (2.14). Impose this over the full sample to recover the structural capacity utilization series: $\hat{\mu}_t = y_t - \hat{\theta}_t^{(1)} k_t$.

The decomposition achieved by this procedure is precise. Transitory distributional fluctuations — the component $\pi_t - \hat{\pi}_t^{LR}$ purged from the technology channel — feed $\hat{\mu}_t$ as demand-side variation, exactly where the Weisskopf decomposition requires them to appear. The Okishio crisis trigger, which in Chapter 1 operates through the interaction of the profit share and capital productivity, passes through this channel: a secular compression of $\hat{\pi}_t^{LR}$ lowers $\hat{\theta}_t^{(1)}$ and thereby shifts the capacity manifold, whereas a cyclical profit squeeze of the same magnitude leaves the manifold unchanged and registers only in $\hat{\mu}_t$. The identification does not suppress distributional dynamics — it assigns them to the correct structural channel.

Capital productivity at normal capacity follows directly from the first-stage elasticity. In log levels, $\hat{b}_t = (\hat{\theta}_t^{(1)} - 1)\, k_t$, so that the levels expression for the capacity-capital ratio is $\hat{B}_t = \exp\!\bigl(\hat{\theta}_t^{(1)}\, k_t\bigr) / K_t$. This is the object that enters Stage B of the Weisskopf profitability decomposition and Stage C of the investment function estimation — the supply-side component of the profit rate, purged of demand-side contamination by the long-run projection.

A scope note on the Gonzalo-Granger decomposition is warranted. The GG permanent-transitory decomposition of the four-variable system serves as a robustness diagnostic for the recovered $\hat{\mu}_t$: it permits an independent check on whether the transitory component of capacity utilization aligns with the error-correction residuals of the restricted VECM. However, applying GG to a lower-dimensional system $(y_t, k_t, \pi_t)$ that omits the interaction term $\pi_t k_t$ is not admissible. Abstracting from the interaction eliminates the distributional dependence of $\hat{\theta}_t$, collapsing the identification to a fixed-elasticity case and forfeiting the structural content that motivates the four-variable specification. All permanent-transitory decompositions in this chapter operate on the full state vector. The next subsection extends the cost-minimization framework to the peripheral economy, where the composition of the capital stock and the balance-of-payments constraint reshape the structure of the identification problem.
