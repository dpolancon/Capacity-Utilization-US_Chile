## 1. GOVERNING REGISTER

§2.2 is a **crisis-theory literature review that carries theoretical work**. It is not
a survey. Every paragraph must advance the argument — either by establishing a level
of the crisis-trigger ladder or by positioning the chapter against a rival claim.

The register is **analytical-historical**: present tense for theoretical claims and
mechanisms, past tense for historical events and the conjuncture that produced the
interventions being reviewed. The prose is assertive, verdict-driven, and compressed.
It does not narrate what the section does; it constitutes the object under analysis.

## 4. SENTENCE ARCHITECTURE

### 4.1 Object-constitution openers (C6)

Every subsection opens by constituting the object, not by announcing the topic.

**Wrong:** "This subsection reviews the post-Keynesian literature on desired
capacity utilization."

**Right:** "The question of whether firms hold a stable desired capacity
utilization rate has roots deeper than the Kaleckian model."

**Wrong:** "§2.2.3 discusses Okishio's concept of the crisis trigger."

**Right:** "The classical long-run closure — the accumulation equilibrium
trajectory in which realized output matches productive capacity — requires
conditions so stringent that Marx's critique of the classics amounts to
denying its feasibility as anything other than a logical benchmark."

### 4.2 Verdict coupling (C1)

Evidence and verdict must appear in the same paragraph. Use "therefore,"
"that is why," or the not-X-but-Y construction as connectors.

**Wrong:**
> Para 1: Vidal shows management is divided between coordination and discipline.
> Para 2: Therefore the firm cannot hold a coherent utilization target.

**Right:**
> Vidal shows management is divided between coordination and discipline — the
> firm must simultaneously extract disciplined output and mobilize workers'
> cognitive capacities. There is therefore no coherent utilization target to
> anchor investment plans — not because of competitive pressure between firms,
> but because of constitutive contradictions within the firm.

### 4.3 Catalogue prohibition (C3)

No paragraph may list more than three named scholars without each doing
distinct analytical work in the sentence where they appear. Block citations
(Kalecki 1971; Steindl 1952; Dutt 1990; Bhaduri and Marglin 1990) are
acceptable when the authors share a common premise and the paragraph's
work is to identify that premise, not to distinguish the authors.

### 4.4 Exit ramp sentences (C7)

Final sentence of each subsection names why the next move is historically
or theoretically necessary — not what it covers.

**Wrong:** "The next subsection discusses Okishio's crisis trigger."

**Right:** "That the limit of capitalist accumulation is capitalism itself
does not yet specify the mechanism. That is why the crisis trigger must be
located in the path of capacity utilization — the variable that registers
the deviation of actual accumulation from the equilibrium trajectory."

### 4.5 Em-dash appositives

Diego's signature construction. Use freely — target ~300/1000 sentences.
The em-dash introduces a qualification, redefinition, or compression
within the sentence without subordination.

> "The Fordist accumulation regime — whose demise this chapter tests
> empirically — was the very object whose breakdown prompted their
> theoretical interventions."

> "Spare capacity is not waste; it is the form in which firms internalize
> the anarchic character of market demand."

### 4.6 Sentence length

- Mean: ~30 tokens. Vary between 15 (verdict punch) and 50 (complex
  qualification with em-dash appositive).
- Verdict sentences: short. ≤ 35 tokens.
- Context-setting sentences: can run longer with subordinate clauses.
- Never exceed 55 tokens.

## 5. PARAGRAPH ARCHITECTURE

### 5.1 Internal structure

Each paragraph follows one of two templates:

**Template A — Evidence → Verdict:**
1. State the claim or reference (1–2 sentences)
2. Specify the mechanism (1–2 sentences)
3. Deliver the verdict with connector (1 sentence, short)

**Template B — Object-constitution → Complication → Resolution:**
1. Constitute the object: "X is not what it appears" (1 sentence)
2. Complicate: "because Y" (1–2 sentences)
3. Resolve: "therefore Z" or "that is why" (1 sentence)

### 5.2 Paragraph length

- Target: 4–6 sentences per paragraph.
- Never exceed 8 sentences.
- Single-sentence paragraphs permitted only for punchline/verdict isolation.

## 6. MOVE-LEVEL CONSTRAINTS

### §2.2.1 — Crisis as the Limit of Capitalist Accumulation
- **Opens with:** Shaikh constituting the object (crisis-prone capitalism)
- **Closes with:** exit ramp to §2.2.2 — why the mode-of-production level
  does not yet specify the mechanism
- **Historicization paragraph:** grounds Shaikh/Itoh in the conjuncture
  (crisis of Fordism, global upheaval, re-ignition of Marxist crisis theory)
- **Shaikh vs. Itoh:** Shaikh forecloses disproportionality; Itoh preserves
  theoretical space for demand-side causation. This distinction is load-bearing.

### §2.2.2 — Disproportionality, Class Struggle, and Spatial Fixes
- **Arrighi:** disproportionality as falling profitability, two forms.
  Concentration/centralization as crisis accelerator.
- **Harvey:** spatial AND temporal fixes made explicit. Credit as temporal fix.
- **Closes with:** Fordist Λ as the most extensive spatio-temporal fix.
  Exit ramp: the fix does not name the trigger mechanism.

### §2.2.3 — Okishio's Crisis Trigger
- **Opens with:** object-constitution of the classical long-run closure as
  knife-edge, not attractor. Marx's critique operationalized.
- **No "normal level" language.** Reference point is the accumulation
  equilibrium trajectory.
- **Demand-causal chain** through Itoh's taxonomy: overaccumulation and
  underconsumption as two expressions of the failure to sustain the
  accumulation equilibrium trajectory.
- **μₜ grounded** in lifted classical closure, not in production-structure
  formalism (which comes in §2.3).
- **Weisskopf bridge:** displayed equation r = μ·B·π. Demand-led profitability.
  Identification problem stated: structurally identified μ̂ₜ needed.
- **Closes with:** exit ramp to §2.2.4 — the crisis trigger is established
  at the aggregate level but requires a firm-level foundation.

### §2.2.4 — The Firm and Market Anarchy
- **Move 1:** PK desired-CU position. Ciccone (1986), Steindl (1952),
  Kalecki (1971), Dutt (1990), Bhaduri-Marglin (1990), Skott (2012),
  Nikiforos (2013, 2016), Serrano (1995). Block citations where authors
  share common premise. Pivot: "these contributions share a common
  premise — it is this premise that the present chapter challenges."
- **Move 2:** Critique at two levels. Macro: market anarchy (Okishio §2.2.3).
  Micro: Vidal (2022) management contradiction + workforce contradiction.
  Punchline: "the firm does not overcome market anarchy; it internalizes it
  through constrained planning shaped by uncertainty and contradictory
  interests across organizational scales."
- **Move 3:** Aggregation failure. No within-firm target → macro μ̄ has no
  behavioral referent. What aggregates is the conflict.
- **Move 4:** Fordist resolution (institutional suppression of within-firm
  contradiction) + scale of accumulation (nationally bounded under Bretton
  Woods vs. globalized under post-Fordism). Arrighi on concentration/
  centralization rescaled. Working class bargaining position structurally
  weakened. Weisskopf μ̂ₜ reading: not a collective choice of lower target
  but withdrawal of institutional conditions + rescaling of accumulation.
- **No empirical tests, no Stage C apparatus, no CUSUM, no ψ₃.**

### §2.2.5 — Synthesis
- **Four-level ladder table.** One mechanism per level.
- **Λ as the settlement operating across all four levels.**
- **Transition sentence to §2.3:** names why the analytical framework is
  historically necessary — not what it covers.
- **No Duménil-Lévy or Basu in this table** (they belong in §2.3.1).

## 7. CITATION DISCIPLINE

- **Running text:** only when the author does distinct analytical work in
  the sentence. "Ciccone (1986) reconciles..."
- **Block citations:** when authors share a common premise or finding.
  "(Kalecki 1971; Steindl 1952; Dutt 1990; Bhaduri and Marglin 1990)"
- **Never stack more than 5 references** in a single parenthetical.
- **Author-year format** throughout. No footnote numbers, no brackets.
- **Vidal rule:** Vidal (2019) = crisis theory, stagnation, periodization.
  Vidal (2022) = firm-level contradictions, management divided. Never
  conflate. Vidal (2014) = institutional incoherence (distinct contribution).
