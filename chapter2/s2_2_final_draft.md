## §2.2.1 Crisis as the Limit of Capitalist Accumulation

For Shaikh (1978), capitalism is inherently crisis-prone: the circuit of capital is structurally exposed to interruption because the anarchy of production permits no social coordination of aggregate supply and demand. Demand stimulus displaces crisis temporally — it defers the reckoning to a future horizon and enlarges the magnitude of the eventual reversal — but cannot resolve the underlying contradiction. The limit of capitalist accumulation is capitalism itself. Critically, Shaikh rejects disproportionality as a source of crisis, treating the falling rate of profit as the singular tendential law that governs long-run capitalist dynamics — a position he maintains and crystallizes in his later work (Shaikh 2016).

Itoh (1978) shares Shaikh's starting point — crisis arises from the competitive processes intrinsic to the capitalist mode of production — but arrives at a more open-ended account. He distinguishes two forms without subordinating one to the other. An *excess of capital* crisis (overaccumulation) occurs when capital accumulates beyond profitable employment — the circuit cannot expand fast enough to absorb the surplus. An *excess commodity* crisis (underconsumption or realization failure) occurs when insufficient aggregate demand prevents realization of the produced surplus value. Both are endemic; neither precludes the other; and both can occur simultaneously at different points in the circuit. Where Shaikh forecloses the underconsumptionist channel, Itoh preserves the theoretical space for demand-side causation — a distinction that matters for this chapter's reading of $\mu_t$ as a crisis trigger operating through both channels.

Both interventions were products of a specific historical conjuncture. The crisis of the Fordist accumulation regime was unfolding in real time: the collapse of the Bretton Woods monetary order in 1971, the first oil shock in 1973, and the onset of stagflation had shattered the postwar consensus on capitalist self-regulation. Simultaneously, the global upheaval of the late 1960s — the May 1968 uprisings, the wave of revolutionary movements across Latin America, decolonization struggles in Africa, and the emergence of the anti-war, anti-poverty, full-employment coalition in the United States — had re-ignited Marxist scholarly capacities for crisis analysis after a long period of marginalization during the postwar boom. Shaikh and Itoh were not theorizing in a vacuum; they were recovering and renovating classical Marxist crisis theory in the face of a world-historical rupture that demanded it. The Fordist accumulation regime — whose demise this chapter tests empirically — was the very object whose breakdown prompted their theoretical interventions.

That the limit of capitalist accumulation is capitalism itself does not yet specify the mechanism through which crisis is displaced, deferred, or triggered. That is why the analysis must move from the mode of production to the meso-institutional and spatial forms through which crisis is managed — and eventually to the variable that registers its failure.

## §2.2.2 Disproportionality, Class Struggle, and Spatial Fixes

Arrighi (1978) analyses crisis as falling profitability through disproportionality — not as a single mechanism but as two complementary forms. First, downward pressure on wages produces demand deficiency and a realization crisis. Second, upward pressure on wages produces profit squeeze and an accumulation bottleneck. Concentration and centralization of capital (Marx, KP VI) accelerate during crisis — strengthening collective capitalist power but simultaneously concentrating working-class leverage. The state absorbs excess capital through unproductive expenditures — military, social reproduction — without resolving the underlying distributive contradiction.

Harvey (2001, 2018) introduces the concept of spatio-temporal fixes: crisis is displaced across space and deferred across time through investment in built environments and geographical restructuring. The spatial dimension operates through geographical reallocation of capital and labour; the temporal dimension operates through credit and debt instruments that bridge gaps between production and realization, converting present crisis into future obligation. Three cuts of crisis are identified: (i) overaccumulation and underconsumption as cyclical phases of the circuit; (ii) the credit system bridging temporal gaps between production and realization; (iii) geographical reallocation displacing crisis to other spaces and future horizons (Luxemburg 2015; Poulantzas 2000; Trotsky 2017). The Fordist accumulation regime constitutes, from this perspective, the most successful and geographically extensive spatio-temporal fix in the history of capitalism — temporarily displacing its endemic contradictions across both dimensions simultaneously.

But spatio-temporal fixes displace crisis; they do not name its trigger. That is why the analysis must turn to the variable through which accumulation dynamics produce stagnation and reversal — and to the theorist who first identified it.

## §2.2.3 Okishio's Crisis Trigger: Cumulative Causation through Capacity Utilization

The classical long-run closure — the accumulation equilibrium trajectory in which realized output matches productive capacity, saving equals investment, and the economy reproduces itself smoothly — requires conditions so stringent that Marx's critique of the classics amounts to denying its feasibility as anything other than a logical reference point (Basu 2019). Okishio (1961; 2022) operationalizes this critique. The accumulation equilibrium trajectory is not an attractor but a knife-edge: the decentralized, profit-oriented decisions of individual capitalists cannot sustain the coordination required to keep effective demand aligned with productive capacity over the long run. The deviation of actual accumulation from the equilibrium trajectory is systematically self-reinforcing, not self-correcting. Capacity utilization — the ratio of effective output to productive capacity, $\mu_t = Y_t / Y_t^p$ — is the variable that registers this deviation, and its cumulative path is what Okishio calls the *crisis trigger*.

The crisis trigger operates through the demand-causal chain connecting the two Itoh forms. When profit-oriented capitalist behavior drives expansion of productive capacity beyond what effective demand can absorb, $\mu_t$ rises cumulatively — each firm's investment adds capacity that collectively outpaces the growth of realized demand. The overaccumulation builds until inventories accumulate, asset values collapse, and the herd behavior of capitalists simultaneously withdrawing from accumulation produces a violent contraction. This is the *excess of capital* expression: crisis triggered by cumulative overshooting of $\mu_t$ above the accumulation equilibrium trajectory. Conversely, when wage suppression and cost-cutting erode the consumption component of aggregate demand, $\mu_t$ declines secularly — the productive base remains in place but cannot be activated because purchasing power has been systematically compressed. This is the *excess of commodities* expression: a smoother, more protracted realization crisis in which the accumulation equilibrium trajectory recedes as effective demand falls further behind productive capacity (Okishio 1961; Itoh 1978).

In both expressions, $\mu_t$ is not an unconstrained parameter. The classical long-run closure requires that effective demand match productive capacity — that the economy reproduce itself smoothly along the accumulation equilibrium trajectory. When this closure is imposed, $\mu_t = 1$ by construction and the realization problem disappears. The chapter lifts that closure. Once smooth reproduction is no longer assumed, $\mu_t$ emerges as the structurally determined gap between what effective demand actually realizes and what the productive base could deliver. The crisis trigger is therefore not a deviation from an equilibrium that the economy tends toward, but the expression of a structural disequilibrium that the classical long-run closure suppresses.

This connects directly to the Weisskopf (1979) decomposition of the profit rate,

$$r_t = \mu_t \cdot B_t \cdot \pi_t,$$

which makes profitability demand-led: $\mu_t$ scales the product of capital productivity at normal capacity $B_t$ and the profit share $\pi_t$. If $\mu_t$ follows a cumulatively unstable path — as the Okishio argument implies whenever the classical long-run closure fails — the profit rate inherits that instability even when $B_t$ and $\pi_t$ are stationary or slowly moving. Reading the Weisskopf decomposition empirically therefore requires a structurally identified $\hat{\mu}_t$ — one that is not imposed by a filter or a balanced-growth assumption — which is precisely what Stage A of this chapter recovers.

But the crisis trigger as stated operates at the aggregate level — it tells us that the path of $\mu_t$ is cumulatively unstable, not why individual capitalist firms are incapable of coordinating around a stable utilization rate. That is why the argument must now move inside the firm.

## §2.2.4 The Firm and Market Anarchy: Multi-Scalar Contradictions Rule Out a Unified Desired Capacity Utilization

The question of whether firms hold a stable *desired* capacity utilization rate has roots deeper than the Kaleckian model. Ciccone (1986) reconciles the classical tradition of long-period gravitational equilibrium — in which profit rates equalize across sectors at normal capacity — with Steindl's (1952) insight that firms deliberately maintain spare capacity as a buffer against demand uncertainty. The normal rate of utilization is, in this account, not an arbitrary convention but a structural feature of competitive capitalist production: it is the utilization rate consistent with the uniform profit rate toward which market prices gravitate. Spare capacity is not waste; it is the form in which firms internalize the anarchic character of market demand.

The canonical Kaleckian model builds on this foundation. Firms set a target utilization rate $\bar{\mu}$ anchored in the markup and competitive conditions; investment responds to the gap between actual and desired utilization; the economy settles at the desired rate in the long run (Kalecki 1971; Steindl 1952; Dutt 1990; Bhaduri and Marglin 1990). A persistent difficulty is that the canonical model cannot reconcile the actual and normal rates in equilibrium — the Harrodian instability problem — which has generated a range of responses: critiques of the investment function's behavioral plausibility (Skott 2012), endogenization of the normal rate so that $\bar{\mu}$ adjusts to the actual rate (Nikiforos 2013, 2016), and alternative closures through autonomous demand (Serrano 1995). Despite their differences, these contributions share a common premise: that the firm is a sufficiently unified planning subject to hold, pursue, or adjust toward a coherent utilization target. It is this premise that the present chapter challenges.

The challenge operates at two levels. At the macro level, the market is anarchic because social production is not consciously coordinated. No mechanism aggregates the decentralized decisions of individual capitalists into a demand path that would sustain any particular $\mu_t$. This is the Okishio point established in §2.2.3: the accumulation equilibrium trajectory is a knife-edge, not an attractor.

At the micro level, the firm itself is not a unified decision-body. Following Vidal (2022), capitalist management confronts two constitutive contradictions of the labour process. The *management contradiction* is the conflict between coordination — organizing production to exploit economies of scope, learning, and flexibility — and discipline — maintaining control over the valorisation process and ensuring that workers produce surplus value. The *workforce contradiction* is the tension between productive socialisation — the development of cooperative capabilities, problem-solving, and engagement — and alienation — the structural exclusion of workers from the purposes and benefits of their own productive activity. Both contradictions pull the utilization decision in incompatible directions: coordination and productive socialisation favour sustained high utilization that develops workers' capacities; discipline and valorisation pressure favour intensification, cost-cutting, and the maintenance of excess capacity as a disciplinary reserve. These pressures operate across incommensurable organizational scales and temporal horizons — from shop-floor struggles over routine, pace, and problem-solving access (Vázquez 2006), through plant-level tactical responses to bottlenecks and product-mix complexity (Anderson 2001), to corporate strategies subordinated to shareholder-value extraction and financialized time horizons (Lazonick and O'Sullivan 2000). They do not converge on a single $\bar{\mu}$. The firm does not overcome market anarchy; it internalizes it through constrained planning shaped by uncertainty and contradictory interests across organizational scales.

If individual firms do not possess coherent utilization targets, the standard aggregation fails at the source. The macro variable $\bar{\mu}$ that enters the post-Keynesian investment function has no behavioral referent. What aggregates is not a representative firm's target but the distributive struggle within each firm over how much capacity to activate, under what conditions, and for whose benefit. Aggregate $\mu_t$ is therefore a residual of unresolved contradictions interacting through market competition — not a deviation from a well-defined center of gravity.

The Fordist accumulation regime temporarily stabilized the within-firm contradiction. High effective demand sustained by the consumption norm, a regulated wage relation indexed to productivity, and a Taylorist organisation of the labour process that suppressed the management contradiction by separating conception from execution — all of these institutionally aligned the competing imperatives sufficiently to produce a *de facto* normal $\mu$. This was not because the firm became internally unified, but because the external institutional settlement narrowed the terrain of organizational conflict: the management contradiction was muted under Fordist work organization, and the workforce contradiction was partially absorbed by rising real wages and employment security (Vidal 2019).

Crucially, the Fordist settlement was nationally bounded. The Bretton Woods monetary order permitted national policy autonomy — Keynesian demand management, regulated wage bargaining, administered pricing — precisely because capital accumulation was contained within national circuits. The international financial architecture constrained cross-border capital mobility, which meant that the distributive compromise between capital and labour could be enforced at the national scale without being arbitraged away by capital flight or global sourcing. The scale of accumulation and the scale of institutional regulation coincided, and that coincidence was a condition of the settlement's capacity to suppress the within-firm contradictions described above.

The demise of the Fordist settlement re-releases both contradictions simultaneously — at the firm level and at the scale of accumulation. Under post-Fordist production, the basis of efficiency shifts to economies of scope and flexibility, intensifying the management contradiction: coordination requires worker engagement but valorisation requires discipline, and these pull harder in opposite directions than they did under Taylorism (Vidal 2019; Vidal 2022). At the same time, capital accumulation is unleashed from its national containers. The concentration and centralization of capital — tendencies that Arrighi (1978) identified as crisis accelerators — now operate through global value chains of production that distribute the labour process across national jurisdictions with radically different wage levels, labour disciplines, and institutional settlements. Spatio-temporal fixes that had previously displaced crisis within and across national economies now operate at a planetary scale, opening new spaces of accumulation while generating new contradictions and crisis tendencies in other spaces and future temporal horizons (Harvey 2001). The working class's bargaining position is structurally weakened: the national institutional framework that had sustained its leverage — regulated wage bargaining, full employment policy, restrictions on capital mobility — is dismantled precisely as the concentration of capital strengthens the collective power of an increasingly transnational capitalist class. What the Weisskopf decomposition records as a falling $\hat{\mu}_t$ in the late 1960s is therefore not reducible to the firm level alone. It registers the simultaneous withdrawal of the institutional conditions that had suppressed the within-firm contradiction *and* the rescaling of accumulation beyond the reach of the national settlements that had contained it.

## §2.2.5 Synthesis: A Multi-Level Theory of the Crisis Trigger

The four strands of the preceding analysis produce not a catalogue of crisis theories but a coherent ladder — each level specifying a distinct mechanism, and the Fordist accumulation regime operating as the institutional settlement that held all four simultaneously.

| Level | Mechanism | Reference |
|---|---|---|
| Mode of production | Crisis is endemic; demand stimulus displaces, not resolves | Shaikh (1978); Itoh (1978) |
| Meso/spatial | Spatio-temporal fixes displace crisis across space and time | Arrighi (1978); Harvey (2001, 2018) |
| Aggregate/macro | $\mu_t$ path is the crisis trigger; cumulative disequilibrium | Okishio (1961, 2022) |
| Firm/organizational | Multi-scalar contradictions rule out a stable desired $\mu$ | Vidal (2022) |

The Fordist accumulation regime is the institutional settlement that operated across all four levels simultaneously — holding the class struggle within distributable bounds, stabilizing the spatial fix within nationally bounded circuits of accumulation, suppressing within-firm contradiction through Taylorist work organization and the consumption norm, and keeping $\mu_t$ near enough to the accumulation equilibrium trajectory that stagnation tendencies offset rather than reinforce. The chapter tests empirically what happens when that settlement breaks.

To make the crisis-trigger hypothesis testable, $\mu_t$ must be identified structurally — not imposed by a filter or a balanced-growth assumption. That is why the chapter now turns to the analytical framework that formalizes the production structure, the accounting scaffold, and the stagnation typology through which the three empirical stages are organized.
